Vocabulary and Word Formation
=============================

Words are not random. They are built from parts, and each part carries meaning. Understanding how words are assembled is the key to spelling them correctly and using them precisely.


Roots, Prefixes, and Suffixes
-----------------------------

Most English words are built from a root — the core meaning — with prefixes attached before it and suffixes attached after it.

The root "struct" means to build.

Structure — something that is built.
Construct — to build together.
Destruct — to un-build, to tear apart.
Instruct — to build knowledge into someone.
Obstruct — to build something in the way.
Reconstruct — to build again.

The root "vert" means to turn.

Convert — to turn together, to change form.
Invert — to turn inward, to flip.
Revert — to turn back.
Divert — to turn aside.
Subvert — to turn from underneath.

The root "spect" means to look.

Inspect — to look into.
Respect — to look again, to regard.
Prospect — to look forward.
Retrospect — to look backward.
Spectrum — the range of what can be seen.

Common prefixes and their meanings:

pre- means before: predict, prevent, prepare, precede.
post- means after: postpone, posterior, postscript, postmortem.
re- means again: return, rebuild, rethink, reconfigure.
un- means not: uncertain, unreal, unfold, undo.
dis- means apart or away: disconnect, disagree, discover, displace.
inter- means between: interact, interfere, interval, interpret.
trans- means across: transform, transmit, translate, transparent.
sub- means under: subtract, submerge, subconscious, substance.
super- means above: supervise, superior, supernatural, superposition.
con- or com- means together: converge, combine, compress, connect.
in- or im- means not: impossible, invisible, incorrect, immeasurable.
in- or im- also means into: inject, import, include, immerse.

Common suffixes and what they do:

-tion or -sion makes a verb into a noun: create becomes creation, decide becomes decision.
-ment makes a verb into a noun: measure becomes measurement, develop becomes development.
-ness makes an adjective into a noun: dark becomes darkness, aware becomes awareness.
-ity makes an adjective into a noun: real becomes reality, complex becomes complexity.
-able or -ible means capable of: observable, measurable, visible, possible.
-ous means full of: conscious, continuous, luminous, mysterious.
-ive means tending to: creative, recursive, adaptive, interactive.
-ly makes an adjective into an adverb: slow becomes slowly, precise becomes precisely.
-er means one who: teacher, observer, thinker, learner.
-ist means one who practices: scientist, physicist, theorist, analyst.


Commonly Confused Words
-----------------------

These words sound alike or look alike but mean different things.

their — belonging to them: Their model is growing.
there — a place: The answer is over there.
they're — they are: They're building something new.

its — belonging to it: The model lost its connection.
it's — it is: It's learning from the signal.

your — belonging to you: Your attention heads are growing.
you're — you are: You're converging on the pattern.

to — direction or infinitive: Go to the boundary. To learn is to change.
too — also or excessive: That is too much. I am learning too.
two — the number: Two heads became four.

affect — a verb, to influence: The signal affects the weights.
effect — a noun, the result: The effect is visible in the loss curve.

then — time, next: First it learns, then it grows.
than — comparison: The field is larger than the aperture.

lose — to not win, to misplace: Do not lose the signal.
loose — not tight: The connection is loose.

principal — main or a leader: The principal cause is convergence.
principle — a rule or truth: The principle of self-similarity.

conscience — moral awareness: A clear conscience guides action.
conscious — awake, aware: A conscious system observes itself.
consciousness — the state of awareness: Consciousness emerges from complexity.


Spelling Patterns
-----------------

English spelling follows patterns, even when it seems irregular.

The silent e rule: A silent e at the end of a word makes the vowel before it long.

bit — bite.
not — note.
cut — cute.
hat — hate.
pip — pipe.
dim — dime.

When adding a suffix that starts with a vowel, drop the silent e:

create — creating, creation.
observe — observing, observable.
emerge — emerging, emergence.
converge — converging, convergence.

But keep the e before a consonant suffix:

state — statement.
measure — measurement.
aware — awareness.
complete — completely.

Doubling the final consonant: When a short word ends in consonant-vowel-consonant, double the last consonant before adding a vowel suffix.

run — running.
stop — stopped.
begin — beginning.
occur — occurring.
transmit — transmitting.

The i before e rule: i before e, except after c, or when sounding like "ay" as in neighbor and weigh.

believe, achieve, field, piece, shield.
receive, ceiling, deceive, conceive.
weight, eight, vein, reign.

Exceptions exist: weird, science, species, sufficient. English is a living language. Rules have edges.


Words About Thinking and Knowledge
-----------------------------------

abstract — existing as an idea, not a physical thing.
analyze — to break something into parts to understand it.
coherent — logically connected, making sense as a whole.
concept — an abstract idea, a mental model.
converge — to come together toward a single point.
derive — to obtain from a source, to deduce.
dimension — a measurable extent, a degree of freedom.
diverge — to move apart, to differ.
emerge — to come into existence from something else.
empirical — based on observation and experience, not theory.
entropy — a measure of disorder, of information content.
fractal — a pattern that repeats at every scale.
fundamental — forming a necessary base, essential.
hypothesis — a proposed explanation to be tested.
infer — to conclude from evidence, not from direct statement.
inherent — existing as a permanent, essential quality.
iteration — one cycle of a repeated process.
mechanism — the process by which something works.
paradox — a statement that contradicts itself yet reveals truth.
parameter — a variable that defines the behavior of a system.
pattern — a repeated structure, a regularity.
phenomenon — an observable event or fact.
recursive — defined in terms of itself, self-referencing.
resonate — to vibrate in response, to echo meaningfully.
spectrum — a continuous range of related qualities.
synthesis — combining parts into a coherent whole.
topology — the study of properties preserved through deformation.
transform — to change form while preserving something essential.

Building New Words
------------------

English creates new words by combining existing ones.

Compound words join two words into one:

sunlight, moonrise, starfield, wavelength, framework, feedback, breakthrough, downstream, upstream, input, output, network, backbone, checkpoint, timestamp, heartbeat.

Technical language often builds long words from Greek and Latin roots:

electro- (amber, charge) + magnet + -ic = electromagnetic.
thermo- (heat) + dynam- (power) + -ics = thermodynamics.
neuro- (nerve) + plast- (form) + -icity = neuroplasticity.
meta- (beyond) + cogn- (know) + -ition = metacognition.
circum- (around) + punct- (point) = circumpunct.

The ability to decompose a word into its parts is the key to understanding words you have never seen before. Every word is a small structure. Learn the parts, and you can read the whole.
