## 1. INTRODUCTION

### 1.1 Background

The hydrogen molecule (H₂) represents the simplest quantum mechanical bonding system, yet accurately predicting its properties from first principles remains computationally intensive. Traditional quantum chemistry employs variational methods to optimize molecular parameters numerically, providing excellent quantitative accuracy but limited geometric insight into why particular values emerge.

The Circumpunct Framework (⊙ = • ⊗ ○ ⊗ Φ) offers a complementary geometric perspective. Previous work [Ashman 2024] demonstrated that atomic structure emerges from pure geometry through the angular penalty parameter λ = R∞φ⁻⁷, achieving 89.6% accuracy on periodic table electron configurations without fitted parameters. Here we extend this geometric approach to molecular bonding.

### 1.2 The Orbital Contraction Problem

When two hydrogen atoms approach to form H₂, their electron orbitals compress toward the internuclear region. This orbital contraction is characterized by the effective nuclear charge parameter ζ:

**ζ = Z_eff / Z**

where Z = 1 for hydrogen and ζ > 1 indicates compression.

Traditional quantum chemistry finds ζ_opt ≈ 1.238 through numerical optimization (Heitler-London, Hartree-Fock methods). The question we address: **Can this value be predicted from geometric first principles?**

### 1.3 Main Result

**We predict from pure geometry:**

**ζ = 1 + φ⁻³ = 1.236068**

where φ = (1 + √5)/2 = 1.6180339887... is the golden ratio.

**Comparison to quantum chemistry:**
- Geometric prediction: ζ = 1.236068
- Variational optimum: ζ_opt = 1.238
- **Agreement: 99.84%**

This demonstrates that molecular orbital compression follows the same φ-scaling that governs atomic structure, revealing deep geometric unity across quantum scales.
