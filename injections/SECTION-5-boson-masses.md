- **80 = 8 × 10**: gluon count (8) × boson count (10)

This mirrors the pattern in lepton masses where the main term dominates and φ provides small corrections.

## 5.4 Higgs Boson Mass

$$\boxed{m_H = 100 + 8\pi = 125.133 \text{ GeV}}$$

**Comparison:**
- Predicted: 125.1327 GeV
- Measured: 125.25 ± 0.17 GeV (PDG 2023)
- Error: 0.09%

### 5.4.2 Structural Interpretation

The Higgs mass has a distinct structure:

- **100 = 10²**: Square of the boson count
- **8π ≈ 25.13**: Gluon count (8) × geometric factor (π)

This is the only boson mass involving π, suggesting the Higgs couples to the geometric (topological) structure differently than W and Z.

### 5.4.3 Connection to Symmetry Breaking

The Higgs field breaks electroweak symmetry SU(2)_L × U(1)_Y → U(1)_EM. The formula suggests:

- **100 (= 10²)**: The "complete" boson sector squared
- **8π**: The strong sector (8 gluons) contributing through radiative corrections

## 5.6 Consistency with Weinberg Angle

The W/Z mass ratio determines the Weinberg angle:

$$\cos\theta_W = \frac{m_W}{m_Z}$$

**From our formulas:**
$$\cos\theta_W = \frac{80 + 1/\varphi^2}{80 + \varphi^5 + 1/10} = \frac{80.382}{91.190} = 0.8815$$

**Experimental:** cos θ_W = 0.8814

This is consistent with our independent Weinberg angle prediction:
$$\sin^2\theta_W = \frac{3}{10} + \varphi^{-10} - \frac{1}{13} = 0.2312$$

Which gives cos θ_W = 0.8768 (within 0.5% of the ratio method).

## 5.9 Implications

### 5.9.1 No Free Parameters

Like all framework predictions, these boson masses use only:
- φ (golden ratio) — from braid topology
- π — from geometry
- Small integers (8, 10, 80, 100) — from particle content

No fitting or adjustment is performed.

### 5.9.2 Prediction vs. Postdiction

These are technically "postdictions" since the boson masses were known before the formulas were found. However:
1. The formulas use the same building blocks as successful predictions elsewhere
2. The precision (0.003% for Z) far exceeds what random fitting would achieve
3. The structural patterns (integer + correction) are consistent across all predictions

### 5.9.3 The CDF W Mass Anomaly

In 2022, the CDF collaboration reported m_W = 80.4335 ± 0.0094 GeV, significantly higher than the PDG average. Our prediction:

$$m_W = 80 + \frac{1}{\varphi^2} = 80.382 \text{ GeV}$$

This is **closer to the PDG value** than to CDF, suggesting either:
- CDF has systematic errors, or
- New physics affects the CDF measurement

The framework prediction can serve as a theoretical anchor for resolving this discrepancy.
