§01

THE MOST PATIENT MYSTERY IN CELL BIOLOGY

In 1986, researchers discovered enormous barrel-shaped particles hiding inside nearly every eukaryotic cell on Earth. They named them vaults—for the cathedral ceilings they resemble—and then spent the next four decades trying to figure out what they do.

Seventy-eight copies of Major Vault Protein snap together into a hollow shell roughly three times the size of a ribosome. Inside: a cavity. Associated components include small vault RNAs, a poly(ADP-ribose) polymerase, and a telomerase-associated protein. The structure is among the most conserved in eukaryotic evolution—over a billion years of preservation—yet knockout mice develop normally. The vaults aren't essential. They persist anyway.

Proposed functions read like a list of everything a cell can do: molecular transport, drug efflux, innate immune signaling, scaffolding for kinase cascades, nuclear pore shuttling, stress response, autophagy regulation. Each proposal captures a fragment. None captures the whole. The field has been describing symptoms of vault activity without identifying the underlying geometry that produces them.

The Circumpunct Framework offers a structural diagnosis: the confusion persists because biology has been studying the barrel while the action is at the hole.
