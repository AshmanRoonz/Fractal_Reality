Designated Intimacy Time

A Program for Two

What This Is

A protected container for genuine contact between two people. Not a therapy session, not a conflict resolution meeting, not a performance of closeness. A space held open by agreement — where something real is at least possible.

No specific activity is prescribed. The intimacy may be conversation, silence, physical closeness, honesty about hard things, or simply showing up and being present together. What matters is the quality of presence, not the form it takes.

The Four Conditions

These are not ideals to aspire toward. They are the conditions that define whether the container is active. All four must be present. The absence of any one collapses the space.

Open

Nothing is pre-filtered out before arriving. Whatever is true may be brought in. No agenda determines in advance what is allowed to matter.

Honest

What is said reflects what is actually true, not what is strategic, safe, or calculated to produce a desired response.

Curious

The orientation is toward discovery. "I wonder" rather than "I know." The other person is treated as someone still being understood, not already categorized.

Respectful of Boundaries

Neither person is obligated to go anywhere they are not ready to go. Holding a boundary is an act of integrity, not rejection. It keeps the space safe for openness.

Structure

Frequency

Once per week, on a mutually agreed day. Consistency matters more than frequency. A predictable container becomes safer over time.

Duration

Minimum 30 minutes, maximum 90 minutes. The container has edges. Knowing when it ends makes it easier to enter.

Initiation

Either person may call it. Either person may request a postponement once without penalty. A second consecutive postponement from the same person is a signal worth naming.

Location

Somewhere private. No screens, no background tasks. Physical presence in the same space. The space itself signals that something different is happening here.

Opening Protocol

How a container is entered shapes what's possible inside it. Take two minutes at the start to do this together:

  1. Arrive. Sit across from each other. Pause before speaking. Let the transition happen consciously — you're moving from whatever else was happening into this.

  2. Check in honestly. Each person says, in one or two sentences, where they actually are right now. Not how they want to seem. Not a reassurance. Where they are. "I'm still a bit wired from work." "I feel nervous about tonight." "I'm here."

  3. Name the container. One person says aloud: "I'm here to be open, honest, and curious — and I'll respect your limits and expect you to respect mine." The other affirms it.

Reciprocity Safeguards

A common failure mode: one person holds the space while the other receives it. The container becomes asymmetric. This is not intimacy — it is caretaking with intimacy's name on it.

These safeguards are built into the design:

Floor Time If one person has been carrying most of the airspace, the other may say: "I want to hear something about you." This is not an accusation. It is a return toward reciprocity.
Receiving Check At the midpoint, each person asks themselves silently: Am I actually receiving anything right now, or am I facilitating? If the answer is facilitation, it's appropriate to say: "I need to bring something too."
Equal Completion The session does not close on one person's resolution. Both people name something real before closing — even if it's unresolved, even if it's small.
Labor Acknowledgment If one person consistently proposes, structures, and initiates — that asymmetry is named, not absorbed. Invisible labor does not become sustainable through silence.

Closing Protocol

  1. Each person names one thing they are taking from this time. Not a summary. Something real that landed — a feeling, a realization, a moment of contact. It can be small.

  2. Each person names one thing that still feels open. Not a problem to solve tonight. Just an acknowledgment that something is still in motion. This normalizes incompleteness.

  3. Close the container. Explicitly. "We're done." A touch if it's welcome. Then both people re-enter ordinary life.

What Disqualifies the Container

These are signals that the container has collapsed — not failures requiring punishment, but honest observations requiring naming.

Performance

Saying what sounds intimate rather than what is true. Warmth deployed strategically rather than felt.

Defensiveness

Responding to honesty with justification, deflection, or counter-accusation. The container becomes a debate rather than a space.

Withdrawal

Being physically present but actually absent — monitoring, waiting out, or tolerating rather than showing up.

Flooding

One person's emotional state consuming the entire space so that the other person's presence becomes irrelevant.

Weaponization

Using what is shared here as material for a later argument, or naming disclosures outside the container without consent.

When a disqualifier appears, either person may name it simply: "I don't think we're in the container right now." This is not an accusation. It is an invitation to return — or to close the session honestly and try again another time.

This space is not a promise that everything will be resolved.
It is a promise that both people will show up
as they actually are,
and leave the other more visible than they found them.
