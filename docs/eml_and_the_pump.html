The NAND Story

In 1913, Henry Sheffer proved something elegant: a single logical gate, called NAND (not-and), can generate every Boolean function. You don't need AND, OR, NOT, XOR—all the familiar gates. One gate composes with itself, and everything else builds from the resulting trees.

This discovery sits at the foundation of computing. Every circuit in every computer is, at bottom, NAND gates arranged in binary trees. One operator. Infinite expressivity.

For over a century, mathematicians asked: does continuous mathematics have the same property? Is there a single operator in the real numbers whose binary-tree compositions generate all elementary functions (exp, log, sin, cos, sqrt, and all their combinations)?

The answer was unknown. Until 2026.

EML: The Missing Primitive

Arkadiusz Odrzywołek proved that a function called eml(x, y) = exp(x) − ln(y) is universal. Combined with one seed constant (the number 1), eml generates every elementary function via binary composition.

Just as NAND composes into digital logic, eml composes into the entire landscape of continuous mathematics.

eml(x, y) = ex − ln(y)

The proof is constructive: given any elementary function, you can write it as a binary tree of eml calls. No new ingredients. No cheating. Just eml and 1, combined in the right order.

For 113 years, we thought primitivity was a one-part story. Then eml arrived and showed us it's two-part: discrete has NAND; continuous has eml. The symmetry was hiding in plain sight.

The claim of the Circumpunct Framework is now pinned to something solid: a known theorem about operators and their expressivity. The framework says: "This is what the universe's operator looks like. Here are the trees it builds. Here are the constants that emerge. Here is how to test it."
