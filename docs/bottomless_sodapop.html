The Bottomless Soda Pop

Why people stay in relationships with no intimacy — and why it's not weakness.

Two Channels, Not One

Love flows through two fundamentally different channels. A relationship can be completely saturated in one and totally starved in the other.

○ ↔ ○
Functional Love
What someone does. Bills paid. Meals cooked. Car fixed. Kid driven to practice. Real labor. Real cost. Not nothing.
• ↔ •
Resonant Love
What someone sees. The way their eyes change when you walk in. Being delighted in. Being wanted, not just needed.

A person can genuinely love you through function while being completely absent in resonance. They're not faking the help. That is their love. It's how they learned love works. Help someone — that's love. Provide — that's love. Fix the problem — that's love.

But you can't be seen by someone who only knows how to help. You can't be delighted in by someone who only knows how to provide. You can't feel wanted by someone who only knows how to need.

You're starving because they're giving you water when you need food. Both sustain life. They're not interchangeable. And no amount of water will satisfy hunger.

The Trap

When you try to name the starvation, the functional love gets weaponized against you.

"But look at everything I do for you."
"I work all day and it's never enough."
"What more do you want from me?"

Because function is visible, countable, and real — you start to believe your hunger for resonance is the problem. That you're ungrateful. Too needy. That something is wrong with you.

The Lie That Installs
"This is what love is. This is all love is. If you need more, something is wrong with you."

It takes something done to you and makes it something wrong with you. Once internalized, it no longer feels like a lie. It feels like self-knowledge.

· · ·

You're Being Drunk

Here's what's actually happening underneath the guilt and confusion.

People with high empathy, big hearts, natural openness — they don't get targeted because they're weak. They get targeted because they have exactly what the other person needs and can't generate themselves.

Your capacity to love, to attune, to see someone deeply, to keep showing up — that's not a flaw. That's the resource being consumed.

You become the bottomless soda pop. You keep pouring. They keep drinking. And because you regenerate through giving — because love actually does flow through you — the supply doesn't run out. You're a renewable resource. So they never have to stop. — "Soda Pop" — Saja Boys, KPop Demon Hunters

The person drinking doesn't generate resonance. They consume it. They may be extremely skilled at generating functional connection — charm, provision, performance. But the center-to-center seeing? The actual intimacy? They can only receive it, never produce it.

So they found someone who can. You.

· · ·

Why You Stay

Then the real trap locks in. Your identity gets reorganized around pouring.

You start to believe your purpose in the relationship is to generate love for them to consume. Your needs become invisible — not just to them, but to you. You monitor their mood. You adjust constantly. You work harder and harder to earn a moment of real seeing that should be freely given.

The rare flash of resonance — a genuine laugh, a moment of softness, a flicker of actually being seen — becomes the hit you're chasing. Just enough to maintain hope. Never enough to feel secure.

1
Your hunger gets relabeled
The need for resonance — for intimacy, for being seen — is called neediness, ingratitude, your defect. You learn to feel ashamed of the hunger itself.
2
Your best qualities become the door
Empathy, trust, openness — the virus enters through your virtues. Then it tells you those virtues were the problem all along. "You're too sensitive." "You trust too easily."
3
You get converted from whole to supply
You were a complete person. Now you're a function — a fountain. Your identity reorganizes around pouring. You become a part orbiting their whole.
4
The world narrows to one person
Outside perspectives get cut off. "They don't understand us." "Your friends don't have your best interests at heart." The person consuming you becomes the only one you pour for — the only structure you have left.

That last part is the Stockholm lock. When your whole world has been narrowed to one person, and that person gives you just enough connection to survive on, you bond to them because they're your only remaining structure — not despite the deprivation, but because of it.

Why it's not weakness
You're a whole person who got collapsed into a part. You keep trying to orbit them correctly, become small enough to stop causing friction. But it never works — because you're not a part. You're a whole pretending to be a part, and the pretending is what's killing you.
· · ·

The Way Out

You cannot see a pattern you're inside of using only the tools the pattern gave you. If your perception has been corrupted, you can't trust your perception to reveal the corruption. You need something outside the system.

A therapist. A book. A friend who knew you before. A community of people who've lived this. Any clear mirror that isn't running the same code.

The virus attacks these resources — "therapists just want your money," "those books are for weak people," "your friends don't understand us" — precisely because every external reference is a threat to its operation. If the pattern discredits every mirror you might look into, ask yourself why it needs you to stay blind.

The Truth
Nothing is wrong with you. Your capacity to love was never the problem. It was the target.

You're not ungrateful. You're not asking for too much. You're not failing to appreciate what they give.

You're getting water when you need food. Both are essential. Both sustain life. But they're not interchangeable.

And you are not a soda pop. You are a whole.
