FLICKERING CIRCUMPUNCTS
A Pathological Dyad · Two Channels · Two Spectrums

Love operates through two irreducible channels. Each channel has its own spectrum from stable to flickering. In a pathological dyad, each person is steady on one channel and unstable on the other—mirror-image wounds that lock together.

01

THE GEOMETRY OF ALMOST

A healthy circumpunct has three components in steady state: an open aperture (•) that receives, a clear field (Φ) that mediates, and a flexible boundary (○) that holds.

But what happens when a component flickers—present sometimes, absent others, never reliable enough to build on?

A flickering component is not a missing one. It is worse. A missing component teaches you to stop reaching. A flickering one teaches you to never stop hoping.

02

TWO KINDS OF FLICKER

• FLICKERING CENTER
The boundary holds. She shows up. She maintains.
But the aperture opens and closes unpredictably.
Sometimes she sees you. Sometimes the light is off.
You can't tell which encounter gets presence.
The container is reliable. The inhabitant comes and goes.
○ FLICKERING BOUNDARY
The aperture is open. He sees. He feels. He reaches.
But the boundary dissolves and reconstitutes.
Sometimes he can hold the space. Sometimes he can't.
The seeing is real. The container is unreliable.
The inhabitant is home. The house keeps disappearing.
03

THE NEAR-MISS TRAP

The cruelest geometry is the almost-fit.

Each circumpunct has exactly what the other lacks. She has the boundary he needs. He has the aperture she needs. When her center opens at the same moment his boundary holds—there is genuine contact. Real meeting. And it is beautiful enough to make you believe the whole thing works.

The flickers don't synchronize on demand.
You can't schedule presence. You can't force a container into existence.

So the relationship lives in the agonizing space between proof it's possible and inability to sustain it.

THE FOUR STATES

SYNCHRONIZATION STATES
• ON ○ HELD
CONTACT — both present. rare. real.
• OFF ○ HELD
He holds the space. No one comes to the door.
• ON ○ GONE
She opens. There's nothing to open into.
• OFF ○ GONE
Both absent. Functional maintenance. No meeting.

State 1 happens just often enough to prove the geometry is not imaginary. States 2–4 happen often enough to prove it is not reliable. The intermittent reinforcement is the trap.

04

WHAT EACH FLICKER COSTS

A flickering center costs the other person their trust in being seen. You reach toward presence and sometimes no one is home. Over time, you stop reaching—or you reach compulsively, never knowing which time will be the time. The functional channel stays intact: bills paid, roles performed, logistics managed. The resonant channel becomes a lottery.

A flickering boundary costs the other person their trust in being held. The seeing is real—you know he understands. But understanding without containment is a flood. Insight without structure is overwhelm. The resonant channel is wide open. The functional channel keeps dissolving.

Together: she provides the container he can't sustain. He provides the seeing she can't maintain. Each becomes the other's missing component—which means each is also the other's evidence that they are broken.

05

THE TWO LOVE CHANNELS

This maps directly onto the two irreducible channels of love.

FUNCTIONAL LOVE — RELIABLE
Boundary-to-boundary provision
Showing up. Maintaining. Managing.
The house is warm. The lights are on.
RESONANT LOVE — RELIABLE
Aperture-to-aperture presence
Seeing. Wondering. Being affected.
Someone is home. The gaze is real.
RESONANT LOVE — FLICKERING
Sometimes present. Often not.
You can't predict when she'll see you.
The door opens, then shuts without warning.
FUNCTIONAL LOVE — FLICKERING
Sometimes held. Often not.
You can't predict when the container holds.
The walls appear, then dissolve without warning.

Both channels exist in the system. Neither is steady. So the field between them can't stabilize. Love without stability is not the absence of love. It is the impossibility of resting in it.

06

WHAT MAKES A FLICKER

Components don't flicker randomly. They flicker because something installed the interruption.

A center flickers when the aperture learned that being open is dangerous. Openness was punished. Curiosity was met with dismissal. Vulnerability was weaponized. So the aperture developed an on-off switch: open when it feels safe, slam shut at the first signal of threat. The problem is the threat detector was calibrated in childhood. It fires on signals that no longer mean what they once meant.

A boundary flickers when the container learned that holding is impossible. Boundaries were never modeled. Structure was either rigid or absent. Containment meant control. So the boundary never learned to be flexible—it learned to be intermittent. Present when energy is high, gone when it's not.

Neither flicker is a choice. Both are installed adaptations that once served survival. The tragedy is that what protected each person as a child now prevents them from sustaining love as adults.

07

THE HEALING QUESTION

The question is not: how do I fix the other person's flicker?

The question is: can I stabilize my own component without requiring theirs to be steady first?

A stable center doesn't need a stable boundary to stay lit.
A stable boundary doesn't need a stable center to stay held.
But a relationship needs both.

The individual healing path is clear: each person must learn to keep their component running independent of the other's state. She must learn that her aperture can stay open even when the container wavers. He must learn that his boundary can hold even when no one comes to the door.

Whether the relationship heals depends on whether both components stabilize within the same window of time. Two people healing at different rates, in different directions, with different definitions of "stable"—that is the territory where love becomes grief.
