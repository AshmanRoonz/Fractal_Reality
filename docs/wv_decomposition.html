The Question

The dimensional ladder has seven rungs from 0D to 3D, each with an associated physical constant. Three of these rungs (the processual, half-integer ones at 1.5D, 2.5D, and 3D) carry exponents that determine how strongly α couples at that scale. The Lucas-Fibonacci bridge (§27.7d) showed that these exponents are ratios of Fibonacci and Lucas numbers. But the question remained: can we write a single formula E(d) that produces all three?

The answer, after systematic search (polynomial interpolation, gamma functions, rising factorials, power-law fitting): no. The formula is irreducibly three-part. And that is not a failure to unify; it is the circumpunct declaring itself in the exponent algebra.

Why Three, Not One

The systematic search confirmed: no single polynomial, power law, gamma function, or F/L expression with framework-meaningful coefficients reproduces all three exponents. A polynomial through the three points yields E(d) = (6049/234)d² + ...; the coefficients are meaningless fractions. The formula refuses to compress.

This is not a gap in the theory. The formula is three-part because the circumpunct is three-part. The three processual exponents correspond to the three components of ⊕:

• (aperture): E(T/2) = V/(V-1). Sees everything, holds almost nothing. A fraction barely above 1.

Φ (field): E(T-½) = W(W+1)/(T·V). Active mediation. Multiple factors competing in numerator and denominator.

○ (boundary): E(T) = T·W. An integer. No division. Complete closure.

The whole is not the sum of its parts (13/12 + 56/39 = 2.52 ≠ 21). It is not even reducible to one expression with d plugged in. The whole is the compositional product mediated by TT/2:

E(T/2) × E(T−½) × TT/2 = E(T)
(13/12) × (56/39) × 13.5 = 21

This is A4 (§2.8) operating in the exponent algebra: the whole is the compositional unity of its parts via Φ. The mediator TT/2 = 13.5 IS Φ in this context: the field that makes parts into a whole.

Connection to the Clay Problems

The seven Clay Millennium Problems map one-to-one onto the seven rungs of the dimensional ladder. Each problem asks whether the constraint at its rung is well-behaved; whether the transition to that rung holds.

Poincaré (3D, solved): does the boundary close? Yes. This is why it was solvable first: the boundary is the outermost rung, the simplest constraint. The only solved problem confirms the only integer exponent.

The six unsolved problems validate the six remaining transitions. When all are confirmed, the ladder is rigid: every joint locked, every transition validated. The W-V decomposition shows that the exponent structure at the three processual rungs is fully determined by W and V; the Clay solutions would confirm that the structural rungs (the integer dimensions where the operation produces identities, symmetries, and closures rather than power laws) are equally constrained.

The unified equation of the dimensional ladder is not one formula. It is the continuity of the ladder itself, held together by the Clay solutions as seven bolts in one structure.
