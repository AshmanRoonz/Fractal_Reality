Should & Curiosity

The axis underneath everything

You’re driving. Someone cuts you off. You feel the surge: they should be driving better. They should check their mirror. They should know better.

Notice what the should does. It replaces the actual person in that car with a picture of how they ought to be. You don’t see a tired parent running late. You don’t see a teenager still learning. You don’t see a person having the worst day of their life. You see a violation of how things should be. And the gap between your should and what actually happened is the engine of your anger.

This is small. It ruins your commute and then it passes.

Now scale it up.

Someone you love says: I need space tonight. They’re not attacking you. They’re telling you something true about where they are.

But a should fires. They should want to be close. They should be available. Needing space shouldn’t be necessary if love is working.

And now you don’t see a person naming a need. You see a rejection. The should sits right where their face would be. You can’t see them through it.

This is the same mechanism as the traffic anger. The same structure. One replaces a stranger with a picture. The other replaces the person closest to you.

Same engine. Different depth of damage.

A should is a picture of reality laid over actual reality.

When the should is light, you catch it. You notice you’re angry at traffic and you let it go. The should was never deep enough to confuse you about what’s real.

When the should is moderate, it’s harder to catch. It runs through certain relationships. You should your partner, your kids, your parents. When someone names it, something registers. You can half-see it. But the pull is strong, and you often snap back to the picture.

When the should is heavy, you don’t know it’s a should anymore. It doesn’t feel like an expectation. It feels like truth. They shouldn’t need space. That’s not an opinion. That’s how things are. The should has replaced seeing.

At the extreme, this is a core structure of narcissism. Not a species of monster. A should installed so deep the person forgot it was a should.

Now here’s the other word.

Curiosity is what happens when you drop the should long enough to notice who’s actually there.

Should is the closed position. You’ve already decided what you’re looking at. The picture is complete. No need to ask. No need to look.

Curiosity is the open position. You don’t know what’s there. So you ask. You listen. You’re willing to find something you didn’t expect.

They can’t operate at the same moment. The moment you should someone, curiosity is gone. You’ve replaced them with your picture of them. The moment you get curious about someone, the should dissolves, because you’re admitting you don’t already know.

There is no third position. You are either open to what’s actually there, or you’ve already decided. Shoulding or curious. Closed or open. At every moment, with every person, including yourself.

This is why curiosity can’t be faked.

You can perform listening. You can perform empathy. You can learn the therapy words, do the workbook exercises, say “I hear you” at all the right moments. None of that requires you to actually be open to what’s in front of you.

But you cannot fake not knowing. You cannot perform openness. You’re either genuinely willing to find something unexpected, or you’re running a should and decorating it.

That’s the test. Not what someone says. What they’re willing to discover.

Someone asks for space. Do you get curious about what they need? Or do you hear a should being violated? That’s the whole difference. Everything else is decoration.

When you should yourself, you do the same violence inward.

I should be over this by now. I should be stronger. I should need less. I shouldn’t feel this way.

Each should lays a picture over who you actually are. The picture says: this is what you’re supposed to look like. And the gap between the picture and the person underneath it is the suffering.

Curiosity toward yourself is: Huh. I’m still hurting. I wonder what that’s about.

Not fixing. Not judging. Not improving. Just looking. Willing to find what’s actually there, even if it’s not what you expected. Even if it’s not what you wanted.

This is the beginning of recovery. Not a program. Not a strategy. The moment you stop shoulding yourself long enough to notice who’s home.

So the whole thing reduces to two words.

The virus is a should installed so deep you forgot it was a should. It tells you how things are supposed to be. It replaces the people in front of you with pictures. It replaces you with a picture. And then it punishes the gap between the picture and reality.

The cure is curiosity. Not as technique. Not as skill. As orientation. A willingness to drop the picture. To ask. To look. To find out who’s actually there.

Including you.
