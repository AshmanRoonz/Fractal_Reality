# Confirmatory Curiosity: A Proposed Construct, and a Differentiated Model of Love Built Around Its Failure

*Working Paper, v2*

## Abstract

This paper does two things. First, it proposes a new construct: confirmatory curiosity, defined as attention directed at another person that operates as model-verification rather than discovery. Confirmatory curiosity is phenomenologically experienced as interest and often received as attentiveness, yet it processes the other person only as evidence for or against a model the perceiver already holds. It is distinguished from its established neighbors: confirmation bias (which concerns belief updating, not the stance of interpersonal attention), correspondence bias (which concerns a specific attributional error), and low openness or low intellectual humility (which are trait-level dispositions rather than a describable mode of attention that any person can occupy at any moment). Drawing on dual-process accounts of person perception, we argue that confirmatory curiosity has a dual determination. Acutely, it is a resource state: what interpersonal attention defaults to when the effortful, corrective stage of person perception is unavailable due to cognitive load, stress, fatigue, or emotional occupation. Chronically, it is maintained by function: the perceiver's model of the other acquires jobs other than accuracy (safety, identity protection, grievance custody, obligation management, among others), such that updating the model costs more than being wrong does. The construct decomposes into three separable deficits (cognitive charity, cognitive effort, and faith in the other's potential), yielding three phenomenologically distinct modes of confirmatory attention: prosecutorial, depleted, and archival. Second, the paper embeds this construct in an integrative model of love developed across attachment research, empathy neuroscience, self-determination theory, and motive-discordance research. In that model, love is treated as differentiated potential rather than foundation; it splits into want-love (mediated by affective empathy) and need-love (mediated by cognitive empathy); and its accuracy is verified asymmetrically by resonance. Confirmatory curiosity is positioned as the upstream failure point of the entire architecture: it is the condition under which want-love degrades into projection, need-love degrades into paternalism, self-knowledge degrades into sincere misattribution of the giver's own wants as the other's needs, and resentment converts from an emotion into a standing occlusion. An anonymized institutional case illustration (a special-education setting in which two years of person-level evidence failed to revise an administrative risk model, culminating in undisclosed confinement) demonstrates the construct operating at organizational scale, including a proposed selection effect whereby moral injury concentrates in precisely the staff who retain exploratory capacity. We propose seven testable predictions, including a load-manipulation paradigm specific to the new construct and a factor-structure test of its three-deficit decomposition, and identify the unresolved tensions the model must survive.

**Keywords:** confirmatory curiosity, exploratory curiosity, love, empathy, dual-process theory, cognitive load, self-determination theory, resonance, self-deception

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 The missing construct

Psychology has extensively documented what happens when people reason badly about evidence (confirmation bias; Nickerson, 1998), when they attribute behavior to disposition while neglecting situation (correspondence bias; Gilbert & Malone, 1995), and when they differ in trait-level openness to other minds (intellectual humility; Leary et al., 2017). What it has not named is the mode of attention these failures share when directed at a person: a state in which one attends to another human being, often visibly and warmly, while processing them exclusively as confirmation or disconfirmation of an already-held model.

We call this confirmatory curiosity. Its defining features are:

1. **It is attention, not inattention.** The perceiver is engaged, asking questions, tracking responses. Something is happening. This is what distinguishes it from neglect and what makes it hard to detect from either side of the interaction.
2. **Its target is the model, not the person.** Incoming information is evaluated for fit against an existing representation. Information that fits is registered; information that does not fit is discounted, reinterpreted, or simply not perceived.
3. **It is sincere.** The perceiver typically experiences themselves as curious and attentive. There is no intent to ignore; the filtering happens upstream of awareness.
4. **It is state-like, not only trait-like.** Any perceiver can occupy it at any moment, and, we will argue, occupying it is substantially determined by available cognitive resources rather than by character.

Its contrast term, exploratory curiosity, is attention whose target is the person: it seeks what is actually there, holds the existing model as revisable, and treats disconfirming information as signal rather than noise. The exploratory/confirmatory distinction exists in research methodology (Rubin, 2022) and in descriptions of information search strategy, but has not, to our knowledge, been formulated as a construct describing the stance of one person's attention toward another. A literature search conducted for this paper found the phrase "confirmatory curiosity" only in unrelated contexts (confirmatory factor analyses of curiosity questionnaires; debates over confirmatory versus exploratory research labels), never as a named interpersonal construct.

### 1.2 Why embed it in a model of love

A construct earns its place by doing explanatory work. We embed confirmatory curiosity in a model of love because love is where its consequences are largest and most legible: the model developed below holds that every distinct failure mode of love (projection, paternalism, sincere misattribution, and resentment's occlusion) routes through the same upstream event, the collapse of exploratory into confirmatory curiosity. If that claim is right, confirmatory curiosity is not one variable among many in relational functioning; it is the common bottleneck.

## 2. Theoretical Background

### 2.1 The automatic default in person perception

Dual-process theories converge on a two-system architecture: a fast, automatic, low-cost process (Type 1) and a slow, deliberate, resource-hungry process (Type 2), with Type 1 as the default that Type 2 can override only when capacity permits (Evans & Stanovich, 2013; Kahneman, 2011). Applied to person perception specifically, Gilbert's (1989) three-stage model of dispositional inference holds that categorizing another's behavior and characterizing their disposition occur automatically and cheaply, while the third stage, situational correction, is effortful and is selectively lost under cognitive load. Related work on intention attribution finds the same structure: the automatic response is to judge behavior as intentional, and the slower process that permits unintentional explanations requires available capacity and drops out when capacity is compromised (Rosset, 2008; Eisenkoeck, de Fockert, & Moore, 2024).

The implication we draw is direct: under load, people do not stop forming impressions of others; they stop correcting them. Attention keeps running, pattern-matching keeps running, and the stage that would let the actual person revise the model goes offline first. This is the mechanism of confirmatory curiosity. It is not a decision to stop discovering; it is what attention looks like when only the cheap stages are funded.

This grounds feature (4) above: confirmatory curiosity is a resource state before it is a character trait. Chronic occupancy of the state may consolidate into something trait-like, but its moment-to-moment determinant is capacity.

### 2.2 Capacity versus foundation in love

Classical attachment theory treats the drive to bond as innate and foundational (Bowlby, 1969). Longitudinal work complicates this: associations between early caregiving and adult attachment are weak and inconsistent across domains, attachment patterns are more malleable in childhood than adulthood, and early experience does not straightforwardly determine adult outcome (Fraley & Roisman, 2019); one summary phrase is that "foundations are not fate." The model below takes the stronger position that the capacity for love is potential rather than foundation: broadly present at birth, requiring differentiation and cultivation to become competence, and capable of remaining undeveloped.

### 2.3 The empathy bifurcation

A well-replicated literature distinguishes affective empathy (automatic, bottom-up sharing of another's felt state, rooted in neural resonance) from cognitive empathy (controlled, top-down inference of another's mental state without necessarily sharing it), and the two are dissociable both neurally and clinically (Zaki & Ochsner, 2012; Axiaq et al., 2025). We propose this bifurcation generates two modes of love. A want is, definitionally, present in the other's felt experience, so it can be resonated with directly: want-love runs on affective empathy. A need is frequently not felt as itself (the need for a corrective truth is felt as unwelcome; the need for a boundary is felt as rejection), so it must be inferred: need-love runs on cognitive empathy. Note the asymmetry this creates with Section 2.1: cognitive empathy is a Type 2-style controlled process, which means need-love is the mode of love most dependent on available capacity, and therefore most vulnerable to the confirmatory default.

### 2.4 Self-determination theory: convergence and a complication

Self-determination theory independently distinguishes autonomy support (honoring preferences, choices, perspective) from competence and relatedness support (structure, care, connection), as separable behaviors with distinct effects (Deci & Ryan, 2000; Grenier et al., 2024). These map closely onto want-love and need-love. But SDT classifies autonomy itself as a basic need (Niemiec, Ryan, & Deci, 2010), which complicates the want/need split: if honoring a preference satisfies a need for self-authorship, want-love may be a species of need-love rather than its opposite. We hold this open (Section 8.2).

### 2.5 Motive discordance and self-opacity

McClelland, Koestner, and Weinberger (1989) established that implicit motives (measured projectively) and self-attributed motives (measured by self-report) consistently fail to correlate within the same motivational domain, a finding repeatedly extended since (Schultheiss et al., 2013). People are, measurably, often wrong about their own motives without being dishonest. This supplies the mechanism for a failure mode the model treats as central: a giver whose self-model is inaccurate can relocate an unrecognized want of their own onto another person and sincerely experience it as insight into that person's need.

### 2.6 Resonance and its limits

Experience-sharing research grounds resonance as a real, measurable signature of accurate attunement (Zaki & Ochsner, 2012). But two facts prevent equating love with resonance. First, resonance is producible by manipulation: cold reading, targeted flattery, and exploitative love-bombing work precisely by generating accurate reads deployed extractively. Second, a 2026 finding shows the subjective experience of being loved predicts relationship satisfaction while the subjective experience of being loving does not (Frontiers in Psychology, 2026), which constrains any model treating unfelt care as fully creditable love (Section 8.3).

## 3. The Construct: Confirmatory Curiosity

### 3.1 Definition

**Confirmatory curiosity** is a mode of interpersonal attention in which the perceiver engages with another person while processing incoming information solely as verification or refutation of an existing model of that person, such that the model, not the person, is the functional object of attention. Its contrast, **exploratory curiosity**, is interpersonal attention in which the model is held revisable and the person is the functional object.

### 3.2 Discriminant boundaries

The construct is distinct from:

- **Confirmation bias** (Nickerson, 1998): a tendency in evidence search and belief updating. Confirmatory curiosity is not a bias in reasoning about propositions; it is a stance of attention toward a person. One can hold beliefs open-mindedly in the abstract while attending confirmatorily to a specific person, and vice versa.
- **Correspondence bias** (Gilbert & Malone, 1995): a specific attributional error (over-attributing behavior to disposition). Correspondence bias is one common output of confirmatory curiosity, not the state itself.
- **Low openness / low intellectual humility**: trait dispositions. Confirmatory curiosity is a state any perceiver enters, with trait factors influencing base rates of occupancy but load and depletion influencing moment-to-moment entry (Section 3.3).
- **Inattention or indifference**: confirmatory curiosity is engaged, often warm, often question-asking. Its signature is not absence of attention but attention whose findings are predetermined.

### 3.3 The resource hypothesis

Following Gilbert (1989) and the cognitive-load literature on intention attribution (Eisenkoeck et al., 2024), we propose:

**The confirmatory default hypothesis.** Exploratory curiosity is the effortful, capacity-dependent mode; confirmatory curiosity is the automatic default. Under cognitive load, stress, fatigue, or emotional occupation, interpersonal attention regresses from exploratory to confirmatory without the perceiver's awareness and without any change in the perceiver's values, intentions, or felt sense of engagement.

Three consequences follow. First, confirmatory curiosity will be near-universal under depletion, making it a poor marker of character and a good marker of state. Second, interventions that moralize it ("you never really listen") should underperform interventions that restore slack. Third, chronic environmental load (poverty, caregiving burden, workplace strain) should produce chronic confirmatory attention within relationships even among the well-intentioned, a testable population-level prediction.

### 3.4 Sincerity and invisibility

Because the filtering occurs upstream of awareness, confirmatory curiosity is sincere: the perceiver experiences interest. Because it produces engagement behaviors (questions, eye contact, responsiveness to fit-consistent disclosures), it is frequently invisible to the receiver as well, detectable mainly through a characteristic signature: the perceiver's model of the receiver never updates, and disclosures that contradict the model produce reinterpretation rather than revision. We suggest the phenomenology on the receiving end, over time, is of being simultaneously attended to and unseen, and we note (without developing here) that this phenomenology is heavily reported in clinical accounts of relationships with high-conflict and controlling partners.

### 3.5 Decomposition: three deficits

Confirmatory curiosity is not unitary. It decomposes into three separable deficits, any of which is sufficient to produce it:

- **Cognitive charity** is the presumption of present validity: the other's perspective, want, or need, as currently held, might be right. Its deficit turns attention adversarial; information is gathered against the person rather than about them.
- **Cognitive effort** is the funding: the controlled, Type 2 capacity required for either presumption to operate on incoming information rather than remain an endorsed value. Its deficit is the load case developed in Section 3.3.
- **Faith** is the presumption of potential: the other is not finished; they can grow, change, and surprise, so any model of them carries an expiry date. Its deficit renders further exploration pointless by construction, since a finished person cannot generate news.

These are independent axes. A perceiver can hold charity without faith (you are valid as you are, and you will never be otherwise), faith without effort (I believe you can change, but I cannot currently attend to how), or effort without charity (fully engaged prosecution). The faith axis also closes an internal loop in the model: P1 holds that love is potential rather than foundation. Faith, in this decomposition, is P1 applied to the other person. The deepest form of confirmatory curiosity is therefore the denial of the model's first proposition about another human being: the judgment that their potential has finished converting and only archive remains.

### 3.6 Three modes

Because the deficits are separable, confirmatory curiosity presents in at least three phenomenologically distinct modes, each with a characteristic experience on the receiving end:

- **Prosecutorial mode** (charity deficit): attention as evidence-gathering against the person. Often highly effortful and intensely engaged, which is precisely what distinguishes it from neglect; but every observation is filed by a judgment already rendered. Received as being cross-examined by someone who appears interested; the phenomenology of walking on evidence.
- **Depleted mode** (effort deficit): attention as autopilot. Charity and faith may both be sincerely endorsed; there is simply no capacity to run them. This is the acute, load-driven case, and it explains how well-intentioned, exhausted people wound intimates without any hostile intention present. Received as talking to someone who is looking at you and not there.
- **Archival mode** (faith deficit): attention as filing. The person is already known; the record is complete; new behavior is either consistent (recorded) or anomalous (explained away). Frequently warm, even affectionate. Received as being embalmed in an earlier version of oneself.

The modes predict different repairs (Section 7, Prediction 6): prosecutorial attention responds to charity practice (structured steelmanning is the direct exercise); depleted attention responds to restored slack and is worsened by moralization; archival attention responds to faith repair, for which incremental-mindset induction (Dweck & Leggett, 1988; Yeager et al., 2019) provides an existing, tested template.

### 3.7 Chronic occupancy: motivated model-maintenance

Load explains episodic entry into confirmatory curiosity. It does not explain habitual residence. For chronic occupancy we propose a functional account: the perceiver's model of the other has acquired a function other than accuracy, such that updating the model costs more, within the perceiver's psychic economy, than being wrong about the person does. Attention then reorganizes to protect the model rather than track the person; this is motivated cognition (Kunda, 1990) operating at the level of interpersonal attention rather than belief. We provisionally distinguish six such functions:

1. **The model as armor.** Where early environments made surprise dangerous, a fixed model of others operates as a safety device: already knowing who you are means you cannot ambush me. Exploratory curiosity requires that surprise be affordable; where it never was, the open stance never consolidates. A coercive variant exists: children punished for accurate perception (families in which noticing the caregiver's cruelty or inconsistency is itself the offense) are trained to attend in exactly the way that confirms the sanctioned model; confirmatory curiosity installed as a survival skill.
2. **Identity economics.** The model of the other can be load-bearing in the architecture of the self: if you are allowed to be competent, I lose my role as the competent one; if you are allowed to have grown, my grievance goes stale. Revising you forces revising me, so attention guards you against update in order to guard me.
3. **Grievance custody.** Resentment's motivated form, distinct from its depleting form: the frozen model keeps the ledger open. Updating the other forgives the debt without its being paid, so attention is actively spent keeping the model frozen.
4. **Obligation management.** Genuinely seeing someone creates responsibility for what is seen; a discovered need is a bill. Low-resolution models of people make low demands. If attention is the moral act (Murdoch, 1970), withholding it refuses the claim before it can be made.
5. **Ontology of persons.** Under an entity theory of persons (Dweck & Leggett, 1988), people are fixed; once a model forms, further exploration has no expected value, and confirmatory attention is simply rational given the ontology. This function requires neither trauma nor motive, only a transmitted belief ("people don't change").
6. **Existential economy.** A finished model is restful; an unfinished person demands ongoing presence. In Buber's (1923/1970) terms, confirmatory curiosity is I-It attention wearing I-Thou clothing: warm, engaged, and directed at the representation instead of the presence.

The common structure across all six is identical: model functionality exceeding model accuracy in the perceiver's economy. The functions also sort, with suspicious neatness, under the three deficits: identity economics and grievance custody block charity (the other's validity carries a cost); obligation management and existential economy block effort (the other's reality carries a bill); armor and the entity ontology block faith (the other's potential reads as threat, or as nonexistent). We flag the symmetry of this two-by-three arrangement as a caution rather than a credential: taxonomies this clean sometimes reflect the carving rather than the territory, and the sorting should be treated as a hypothesis for the factor-analytic work proposed in Prediction 6, not as an established structure.

## 4. The Model: Love as Differentiated Potential

We state the model as seven propositions, with confirmatory curiosity now integrated as mechanism rather than appended as precondition.

**P1 (Originary Capacity).** The capacity for love is present as undifferentiated potential in nearly all humans from early infancy, prior to any ability to distinguish want from need. It is potential requiring cultivation, not a guaranteed foundation, and can remain substantially undeveloped absent differentiating input.

**P2 (Differentiation).** Through socialization, the capacity differentiates into want-love (mediated by affective empathy, oriented to the other's agency and felt desire) and need-love (mediated by cognitive empathy, oriented to the other's wellbeing, including wellbeing the other does not currently want and may resist). Because cognitive empathy is a controlled process, need-love inherits its capacity-dependence.

**P3 (Epistemic Precondition).** Accurate targeting of either mode requires exploratory curiosity about the other, paired with a charitable presumption that the other is capable of a valid want, need, or perspective. Under confirmatory curiosity, want-love degrades into projection (meeting the want the model predicts, which is often the perceiver's own want wearing the other's face) and need-love degrades into paternalism (meeting the need the model predicts, unchecked against the person).

**P3a (Reflexive Curiosity).** The exploratory stance must also be trained on the giver's own wants, needs, and motives. Given documented motive discordance (McClelland et al., 1989), a giver's self-model can be inaccurate without dishonesty; cognitive empathy calibrated against an unexamined self-state risks relocating the giver's own unrecognized want onto the other and misfiling it as the other's need. This substitution is sincere: nothing is concealed, because nothing was accurately perceived.

**P3b (Confirmatory Default).** Exploratory curiosity, outward (P3) and reflexive (P3a), is capacity-dependent; confirmatory curiosity is the automatic default under load, stress, fatigue, or emotional occupation. Failures of P3 and P3a are therefore, in the acute case, resource failures rather than value failures, and their correction requires restored slack, not merely renewed intention.

**P3c (Motivated Model-Maintenance).** Chronic occupancy of confirmatory curiosity occurs when the perceiver's model of the other acquires functions other than accuracy (Section 3.7): safety, identity protection, grievance custody, obligation management, a fixed ontology of persons, or existential rest. Under any of these, updating the model costs more within the perceiver's economy than being wrong about the person does, and attention reorganizes to protect the model. Chronic failures of P3 and P3a are therefore, in the typical case, functional rather than accidental, and their correction requires addressing the model's job, not only restoring capacity. This extends the reflexive question of P3a: the giver must ask not only "what do I actually want here" but "what work is my model of this person doing for me."

**P4 (Resonance as Asymmetric Verification).** Resonance is the behavioral and subjective signature of an accurate read, arriving on different schedules by target: immediately for wants (already felt, directly resonated with) and retroactively for needs and beliefs (recognized only upon correct naming, as a delayed "yes, that's it").

**P5 (Orientation as Separator).** Resonance is necessary but not sufficient evidence of love, because accurate reads are reproducible by manipulation. The separating term is orientation: whether the accurate read is deployed toward the other's flourishing or extracted for the reader's gain. Crossing outward accuracy (P3/P4) with reflexive accuracy (P3a) yields a four-cell taxonomy:

1. Accurate outward, accurate reflexive, other-oriented: love, the clean case.
2. Accurate outward, accurate reflexive, self-oriented: conscious manipulation.
3. Accurate outward, inaccurate reflexive: sincere misattribution; the giver's unexamined want, relocated and offered as the other's need. No concealment exists to detect, because the distortion is perceptual (upstream), not intentional (downstream).
4. Inaccurate outward: simple misperception; a P3/P4 failure rather than an orientation failure.

Cell 3 is, on the model's terms, the most common failure mode in sustained close relationships, and it is generated jointly by P3a failure and P3b regression.

**P6 (Occlusion, not Erasure).** Once developed, either mode of love can be blocked by resentment without being unlearned. The occlusion runs on two engines. The load path (via P3b): resentment, as a standing emotional occupation, consumes the capacity that exploratory curiosity requires, causing attention toward the resented person to default to confirmatory without any active suppression. The custody path (via P3c): resentment also motivates model-maintenance directly, because the frozen model keeps the grievance ledger open, and updating the other would forgive the debt without payment. The two engines are dissociable by intervention: restored slack (rest, load removal, affect repair) should thaw load-driven occlusion but leave custody-driven occlusion intact, which yields only to ledger-closing (acknowledgment, repair, restitution, or genuine relinquishment). In both cases the underlying capacity for love persists beneath an attention pattern that can no longer deliver it, and the freeze is self-perpetuating: a frozen negative model generates interpretations that renew the resentment that maintains both the load and the custody.

## 6. Case Illustration: The Track and the Room

The following case is drawn from a first-person practitioner account (an educational assistant in a public school system, working with a student with complex needs). Identifying details have been altered or omitted. It is offered as illustration of the construct operating at institutional scale, not as empirical evidence for the model; its function is to show that every major component of the architecture is observable in a single, ordinary institutional sequence.

### 6.1 The case

Over a two-year period, the practitioner built an outdoor self-regulation routine for the student around a running track: initially accompanied, then supervised from a fixed checkpoint, until the student ran independent laps as a daily regulation practice. The student received more outdoor time than peers because he needed more, and the administration of that period endorsed the arrangement. Functionally, two years of exploratory attention had been converted into transferred capacity: the student carried the regulation himself.

An administrative change then occurred. The incoming team had not participated in the two years of observation; their first contact with the student was through his documentation and his category (a large, unpredictable student; a staffing and liability concern). Outdoor time was reduced. The reduction destabilized the routine, and the student eventually had a single significant outburst. The outburst was read as confirmation of the category rather than as a consequence of the intervention that produced it. The student was subsequently placed in a small room for the remainder of the year, with social contact reduced to brief daily visits from two peers. His parents were not informed of the arrangement; the practitioner informed them independently after a month. The parents engaged legal counsel; the institution promised remediation; the arrangement nonetheless persisted, and parental concern was gradually managed back down. The practitioner, who had built the system that made confinement unnecessary, was assigned to staff the room.

### 6.2 Reading the case through the model

Each component of the architecture appears in sequence:

- **Model precedence.** The incoming administration attended to a representation (file, category, risk profile) rather than to the child; the representation preceded the child into every decision. Two years of disconfirming behavioral evidence (independent laps, transferred regulation) had no channel through which to revise it.
- **The self-sealing loop.** The category motivated the intervention (reduced time); the intervention produced the destabilization; the destabilization produced the outburst; the outburst was received as confirmation. This is P6's freeze dynamic operating institutionally: the frozen model generated the conditions that produced the evidence that froze it further.
- **Confirmatory attention as architecture.** The small room is the construct rendered physically: an environment organized entirely around the model's prediction, in which the child's actual capacities could no longer be exercised or observed, guaranteeing the model's future unfalsifiability.
- **Status-inverted epistemic access.** The person with the most person-level evidence (the practitioner, lowest in the building's status gradient) had no institutional input channel; the persons with the least direct observation held revision authority. The institution was structurally blinded at exactly the point where it had eyes.
- **Absorption of external correction.** Even legal pressure was metabolized without model revision: promised remediation, no change, gradual re-normalization. Confirmatory systems do not merely resist disconfirmation; they convert challenges into managed episodes.
- **Moral injury and the selection effect.** The practitioner's injury (consistent with perpetration-based and betrayal-based moral injury; Shay, 1994; Litz et al., 2009) arose specifically from retained sight: being required to operate an arrangement whose unnecessity he could directly perceive. Staff for whom the model had fully replaced the child were protected by the replacement. The injury selected for the still-sighted, a dynamic predicting that institutions running on confirmatory attention will differentially burn out precisely the staff who retain exploratory capacity.
- **Horizontal propagation under load.** In a subsequent placement, structurally impossible task assignments (one adult; two children each requiring one adult) produced chronic overload, under which frontline staff defaulted to confirmatory readings of one another and blame traveled laterally between workers failing the same arithmetic, while the structure that assigned the impossibility remained unexamined. This is P3b operating at the collegial level: seeing structure requires exactly the slack the load consumed.

### 6.3 The institutional generalization

Institutions run on models because models scale and persons do not: a file can be transferred between staff, a risk category applied in a single meeting, while two years of person-level observation cannot. Model-based attention is therefore not an institutional pathology but an institutional necessity. The pathology is the absence of a revision channel: a structural arrangement in which no behavior of the modeled person, and no perception of the staff closest to them, can update the model. The case suggests that the practically decisive variable in person-facing institutions is not whether they use models (they must) but whether the people positioned to observe divergence between model and person possess standing to force revision. Where that standing is absent, the institution's attention is confirmatory by construction, independent of the intentions or character of any individual within it.

## 7. Testable Predictions

- **Prediction 1 (Load regression).** Experimentally imposed cognitive load during a structured getting-acquainted or partner-listening task will shift attention from exploratory to confirmatory markers: fewer model-revising follow-up questions, more model-consistent paraphrases of ambiguous disclosures, reduced recall of model-inconsistent details, with no reduction in self-reported curiosity or engagement. The dissociation between behavioral markers and self-report is the construct's signature and distinguishes this prediction from a generic load-hurts-performance effect.
- **Prediction 2 (Resonance latency).** Disclosures of felt wants will produce faster resonance markers (agreement latency, facial affect onset) than accurate namings of unstated needs, which will show a delayed recognition profile.
- **Prediction 3 (Curiosity mode and need-detection).** Confirmatory (versus exploratory) attention, whether measured as chronic tendency or induced by load, will predict lower accuracy in identifying a partner's unstated needs, dissociable from affective empathy scores; want-detection accuracy will be comparatively spared.
- **Prediction 4 (Orientation as moderator).** Among interactions matched for read accuracy (via resonance report), other-benefit-coded interactions will predict longitudinal trust growth; self-benefit-coded interactions will predict initial trust followed by collapse upon discovery.
- **Prediction 5 (Occlusion as load).** Within-person, inducing resentment-relevant rumination before a partner-perception task will produce confirmatory-attention markers toward the partner specifically, exceeding those produced by equivalent-arousal negative rumination about a third party; and experimentally restoring slack (rest, load removal, affect repair) will partially restore exploratory markers without any intervening change in the perceiver's stated view of the partner.
- **Prediction 6 (Factor structure and mode-matched repair).** A psychometric instrument built on the construct will show a three-factor structure corresponding to charity, effort, and faith deficits, dissociable from existing measures of empathy, openness, and intellectual humility; and mode-matched interventions (structured steelmanning for prosecutorial attention, load reduction for depleted attention, incremental-mindset induction for archival attention) will outperform mode-mismatched interventions on exploratory-attention markers.
- **Prediction 7 (Two-engine dissociation).** Within resentful dyads, slack restoration will restore exploratory markers only where occlusion is load-driven; where grievance custody is present (indexed by ledger language and rejection of repair bids), slack restoration will fail, and only ledger-closing interventions (acknowledgment, restitution, or relinquishment) will restore exploratory markers. Practically: the vacation fixes the first kind and not the second.

## 8. Limitations and Open Tensions

### 8.1 Terminology collisions

A popular literature uses "wanting versus needing" relationships for a different phenomenon: dopaminergic incentive circuits versus attachment-anxiety circuits (Berridge & Robinson, 2016). Separately, "epistemic curiosity" already has an established meaning (interest in knowledge acquisition) that overlaps only partially with exploratory curiosity as defined here. Dissemination should disambiguate both.

### 8.2 The autonomy-as-need problem

SDT's classification of autonomy as a basic need threatens the categorical status of the want/need split (Section 2.4). If want-affirmation and autonomy-need-satisfaction measures show substantial shared variance, the model should be revised to a single need-oriented architecture with two subtypes (self-authorship needs versus wellbeing needs that may conflict with self-authorship). This is empirically adjudicable but unresolved.

### 8.3 The being-loved / being-loving asymmetry

The finding that felt love, not loving behavior, predicts relationship satisfaction (Frontiers in Psychology, 2026) implies need-love will underperform want-love on concurrent satisfaction measures, consistent with the model but raising an unresolved normative question: whether concurrent satisfaction is the right outcome variable for a mode of love defined by its orientation to long-horizon wellbeing. Longitudinal wellbeing trajectories are the appropriate test, and no such test yet exists.

### 8.4 Measurement of orientation

Reflexive accuracy (P3a) is measurable in principle via the implicit/self-attributed discordance paradigm, and outward accuracy via resonance (P4). The conscious-manipulation cell remains unmeasurable prior to discovery, by design of the manipulator. The measurement problem has been narrowed from the whole of orientation to its single most adversarial cell, but not eliminated.

### 8.5 State, trait, and the moral residue

P3b's resource framing risks over-correcting into full exculpation. P3c partially restores the moral dimension: chronic occupancy is typically functional, and one bears a different kind of responsibility for what one's model of another person is doing for oneself than for a momentary capacity failure. But this creates a new diagnostic burden the model does not yet discharge: depleted and motivated occupancy present identically in the moment and diverge only under repair (Prediction 7), so attributing chronic confirmatory attention to function rather than load, in any particular case, risks exactly the uncharitable modeling the construct warns against. Additionally, chronic voluntary overcommitment, or remaining in depleting conditions when alternatives exist, reintroduces responsibility for one's own capacity conditions at one remove; the model still lacks an account of this.

### 8.6 Conflict with foundationalist attachment accounts

P1's potential-rather-than-foundation framing conflicts with classical Bowlbian foundationalism. The malleability findings (Fraley & Roisman, 2019) support weakened foundationalism but do not straightforwardly establish the stronger potential-based claim. Acknowledged, not resolved.

---

## 9. Conclusion

The paper's central bet is that a single unnamed construct, confirmatory curiosity, sits upstream of the major failure modes of love: projection, paternalism, sincere misattribution, and resentment's occlusion all route through the regression of attention from discovery to verification. That regression has a dual determination: acutely it is a capacity event (the effortful, corrective stage of person perception losing its funding), and chronically it is a functional one (the model of the other acquiring jobs, safety, identity, custody, rest, that make its accuracy secondary). The construct decomposes into deficits of cognitive charity, cognitive effort, and faith, producing prosecutorial, depleted, and archival modes with distinct repairs. The faith deficit closes the model's deepest loop: P1 holds that persons are potential rather than finished foundation, and the archival mode is precisely that proposition denied about another human being. If the bet is right, the practically important question in a struggling relationship is often not "do they love me" or even "are they trying," but which deficit is running: whether either party currently has the charity, the capacity, and the faith for the only kind of attention under which love can find its target. The model surrounding the construct (potential rather than foundation; two loves on two empathy circuits; resonance as asymmetric verification; orientation as the separator from counterfeit) is offered as a working framework, with seven predictions by which it can begin to be tested and six named tensions by which it can begin to be broken.
