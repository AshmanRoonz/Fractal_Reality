What Is Wholeness?

In plain language.
Ashman Roonz

The Question

What does it mean to be whole?

It sounds like a simple question. It isn't. I have been working on it my whole life.

In 1967, a thinker named Arthur Koestler noticed something important. Look anywhere in nature and you find a pattern: every thing is both a whole and a part. A cell is whole, but it's also part of a body. A body is whole, but it's also part of a family. A family is whole, but it's also part of a community. He called these things "holons." The idea became famous because it's obviously true.

But Koestler's idea didn't go far enough.

Here is the starting point. Three things are true about you right now:

You are whole.

You are part of something bigger.

You have parts of your own.

Koestler gave us those three statements. But he never explained what actually makes something whole in the first place. He told us that wholes exist. He didn't tell us what wholeness is made of (besides parts).

· · ·

What Koestler Missed

Think about yourself for a moment.

You have a body. Arms, legs, organs, cells. The parts you're made of.

You have a mind. Thoughts, feelings, the whole inner world where everything connects.

But there's one more thing. You have a soul. Not in a religious sense necessarily; I just mean the center of your experience. The part of you that's looking out at the world right now. The place where "I am" lives.

All three together are what make you you. The whole: body, mind, and soul, held as one. That whole is the circumpunct.

Koestler gave us wholes and parts. But we are not just wholes and parts. We are not just minds and bodies. We have a soul too, and without it, nothing holds together.

Body Mind Soul

Three things, not two. That's the first fix.

And here's the important move: you ARE the whole thing; body, mind, and soul, held together as one. Your soul is the center of you. Your mind is the field of you. Your body is the boundary of you. The word you names all three, held as one.

And notice what the mind actually does. It's the middle piece. It connects your soul (the center of your experience) to your body (the parts you're made of). Without the mind, the soul and the body couldn't be in contact with each other. The mind is the connection.

What Holds a Whole Together

Koestler never asked a simple question: why does a whole stay whole? Why doesn't it just fall apart into its pieces?

The answer is surprisingly elegant: a whole stays together when being together works better than being apart.

Two hydrogen atoms stick together to form water because being together is more stable than being separate. Stars form because gravity pulling things together is stronger than whatever would scatter them. You exist as one person because having all your cells coordinated is better than having 37 trillion cells each doing their own thing.

Call this the wholeness gap: the difference between being one whole and being a pile of parts. When that difference is there, the whole holds. When the difference disappears, the whole dissolves back into its parts.

But here's the real question: who keeps the wholeness going?

Wholes Held from Outside vs. Wholes That Hold Themselves

A rock holds together. So do you. Both are whole. But there's a huge difference in how they stay that way.

Held from Outside

A rock doesn't do anything to stay a rock. The universe holds it together. Gravity, chemistry, the planet it sits on. The rock is passive. It just is.

Rocks, planets, stars, molecules.

Holds Itself

A cell works to stay a cell. It eats, repairs itself, makes more of itself. It's doing half the work of its own wholeness. The other half is done by its environment.

Cells, plants, animals, you.

Nothing holds itself together all by itself. Not even you. You need food, air, other people, a world to be in. But you also do your own share of the work. That's what being alive means: being wholeness that participates in its own wholeness.

This is the second fix: wholes come in two flavors. Passive wholes (held by context) and active wholes (participating in being held). Life is wholeness that works to stay whole.

· · ·

Wholeness Inside Wholeness

Now we come back to Koestler's insight: wholes contain smaller wholes, and are contained in bigger ones.

But here's what he missed. The nesting isn't just layers stacked on top of each other, each one a different kind of thing. Every single level is the same pattern: center, field, boundary. Soul, mind, body. At every scale.

An atom has a center (nucleus), a field (electron cloud), a boundary (the outer edge). A cell has a center (nucleus), a field (cytoplasm), a boundary (membrane). You have a center (your soul, your "I am"), a field (your mind), a boundary (your body). A family has a center (its shared identity), a field (its relationships), a boundary (who counts as family and who doesn't). A galaxy has a center (a black hole), a field (stars and dust), a boundary (its outer edge).

It's not a coincidence. It's the same pattern, repeating at every size. The same engine running at every level.

Every whole is made of smaller wholes.
Every part is a whole in its own right.

That's what makes the nesting real instead of arbitrary. Koestler said: things are nested. That's true. But the deeper truth is: the same three-part structure repeats at every level, which is why the nesting works in the first place. The pattern is what makes the layers fit together.

The Soul Is the Difference

Here's the insight that takes this from philosophy to something you can feel.

Remember the wholeness gap; the difference between being one whole and being a pile of parts. That difference is what the soul is.

Think of Lego bricks. A pile of Lego bricks on the floor is just a pile. But when a child builds a castle, it's a castle. The bricks are the same bricks. But now there's something more than bricks. There's a castle. A shape with meaning. A unity.

What's the difference between the pile and the castle? The bricks are identical. The difference is the castle-ness. The wholeness. The thing that makes a pile into something more than a pile.

That's the soul.

The soul is the difference between being whole
and being a pile of parts.

The soul is not a substance hidden inside you. The soul is what makes you irreducibly more than your organs and your cells. It's the reason you're a person and not just a walking collection of meat. It's the wholeness that your parts become when they're together as you.

When we say someone has "lost their soul," we don't mean a literal substance got stolen. We mean the wholeness has gone flat. They've become a collection of roles, functions, habits; no longer more than the sum of their parts. And when someone is deeply present, centered, alive; "ensouled" in the old language; you can feel the wholeness. The difference is wide. The soul is strong.

· · ·

The Soul Is Given

But here's a subtlety. Just arranging parts into the right shape doesn't make them whole.

Go back to the Lego castle. Imagine a child arranges the bricks into a castle shape, then walks away. A breeze knocks it over. A month later, nobody remembers it existed. Was it ever really a castle?

The arrangement was there. The shape was there. But it only became a castle when the child saw it as one, named it, played with it, treated it as a whole. The child gave the castle its soul.

Arrangement is not enough. Parts in the right shape are still just parts until something converges on them; recognizes them, attends to them, gives them a center. The soul is given, not just emergent.

And notice who gives it: the greater whole. The child is the bigger whole that the castle exists inside. The castle's wholeness is on loan. Walk away, forget about it, and it's just bricks again.

You're different. You participate in your own wholeness. You give yourself your own "I am" (partially). That's what being alive means. But you didn't generate your own center entirely from scratch. Your soul is also given; by the greater whole you're part of. Half by you, half by what holds you. That's the balance.

So What Is Wholeness?

Wholeness is not a label you stick on something. It's not a property like color or height. It's not "having all your parts." It's what happens when three things come together:

A center (a soul, a point of convergence) that makes this particular whole this one and not some other.

A field (a mind, a relational surface) that connects the center to everything else.

A boundary (a body, a filter) that decides what's inside and what's outside.

A cycle of gathering in, turning, and letting out: breathing, thinking, living.

A gap where being whole is more than being a collection of parts.

A pattern that repeats at every scale, from atoms to galaxies.

All of these are present in every whole. The atom, the cell, you, your family, your planet, the galaxy. Same three pieces. Same cycle. Same gap. Same pattern.

You are a whole: a center (your soul), a field (your mind), a boundary (your body), doing the cycle of breathing in, processing, breathing out, every moment of your life.

You are inside a whole: your family, your community, your species, your planet. Each of those is also a center, a field, and a boundary, doing the same cycle at its own scale.

And you contain wholes: your cells, your organs, your thoughts. Each of those has its own center, field, boundary, cycle.

Koestler was right that you're a whole and a part at the same time. What he missed is why. The reason is that the same three-part pattern (soul, mind, body) runs at every level. That's what makes the nesting hold together. That's what connects the levels. That's what makes you you, and also what makes you part of something bigger, and also what makes your cells what they are.

The soul is the center. The mind is the connection. The body is the parts. And the difference between a whole and a pile of parts is the soul itself.

· · ·

One More Thing

Where does wholeness come from in the first place?

Here's the simplest way to say it. There is one thing; call it energy, call it existence, call it what you like; that is everything. Every whole you can point to is that one thing, folded into a particular shape at a particular scale. A cell is it, shaped like a cell. A person is it, shaped like a person. A galaxy is it, shaped like a galaxy.

Wholeness isn't built from parts that came first. Wholeness is what's always been there. The parts are just what happens when wholeness expresses itself at different scales. The one was always whole. It just had to show up in many ways to prove it.

Wholeness is the pattern the one thing uses
to be everything, at every scale, all at once.

Every cell in your body is whole. Every thought you have is whole. Every moment is whole. You are whole. Your family is whole. Your community is whole. The whole planet is whole. The universe is whole. All the same pattern, all the same engine, all the same thing wearing different faces.

You are not a piece of wholeness. You are wholeness itself, at your scale. That is what it means to be whole.
