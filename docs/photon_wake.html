The Photon Wake

No empty space; no smallest particle; the ball surfs its own field

The Intuition

A boat moves through water and leaves a wake. The wake is not the boat; it is the medium responding to the boat's passage. The wake spreads, reflects, interferes with itself. The boat stays one thing, moving along one trajectory, while the wake carries the wave phenomena.

Apply this to the photon. A photon is a ⊙ with its own inner field (Φ), its own aperture (•), its own boundary (○). As it moves, it does not shed its own field outward; its Φ is internal, part of its wholeness. What it does is disturb the smaller ⊙s that compose the medium it travels through. Those smaller circumpuncts, at scales below the photon, respond to the photon's passage by propagating phase disturbance through their own pump cycles. That propagation is the wake: not the photon's field leaking, but the surrounding medium's ⊙s rippling in response.

At a double slit, the photon-core goes through one slit. The wake (the disturbance carried by sub-photon ⊙s in the medium) goes through both. The wake arrives at the screen and sets up a pattern of constructive and destructive phase. The core is then delivered to a position biased by the wake pattern.

This is the hydrodynamic reading of quantum mechanics. It has experimental proof-of-concept: Couder and Fort's walking droplets (2005 onward) reproduce single-slit diffraction, double-slit interference, tunneling, and orbital quantization using literal bouncing silicone-oil droplets on a vibrating bath. A droplet with a wake behaves quantum-like.

The framework does not need to import pilot-wave theory to get here. The wake picture is forced by two framework premises already in place: (A) no empty space, and (B) no smallest particle.

Why the Wake Exists: No Empty Space

A wake needs a medium. Classical mechanics has water; the framework has Φ. The premise no empty space (A0 + A2) says Φ is continuous at every scale; at every scale there are more nested ⊙s. Vacuum is not absence; it is Φ operating at scales below our measurement threshold. Zero-point energy is the residual hum of those nested apertures pumping below instruments.

So when a photon moves, it does not move through emptiness. It moves through a medium composed of smaller ⊙s, each with their own aperture, field, and boundary. Those smaller circumpuncts respond to the photon's passage by pumping (⊛ → i → ✹) in reaction to it. The collective response of those sub-photon ⊙s IS the wake. It is not extra ontology; it is the medium's own structure doing what circumpuncts do when disturbed: cycling, carrying phase, propagating.

Why the Wake Carries Interference: No Smallest Particle

A photon is a ⊙ at its scale, not a terminal unit. By A2 (fractal self-similarity), every ⊙ contains more ⊙s. Inside the photon, the pattern continues. The wake is not "extra stuff added to the photon"; it is the Φ made of smaller nested ⊙s, which respond to the photon's motion by propagating phase disturbance at sub-photon scales.

"Smaller things following the trajectory" is literal in this ontology. There are always smaller ⊙s; the wake is composed of their collective response; the wake's phase is the phase of those sub-photon circumpuncts pumping in reaction to the photon's passage. The photon's own Φ stays inside; the wake is outside, in the medium.

The Bell Challenge, Briefly

Any ball-plus-medium picture has to survive Bell inequality tests. Entangled photon pairs show correlations stronger than any local model can produce. Walking droplets do not reproduce Bell violations because their wake propagates at finite medium-speed. So a naive local wake picture fails at Bell.

The framework's answer is built into its axioms. A2 says every ⊙ is a fractal of the 1; at sufficient depth, parts share the same field, because there is only one field. Two entangled photons are two ⊙s whose wakes, at sufficient depth, are the same Φ. Their correlation is not a signal sent between them; it is a joint fold in a shared deeper surface. Non-locality is not imported; it is native. The 1 is everywhere at once by construction.

entangled pair: two •s, one shared Φ at depth

This does not violate relativity. No information travels between the two measurement events; what "travels" is the pre-existing identity of the deeper field both wakes inhabit. The Bell correlation is the signature of that shared identity.

Derivation: The Bell Correlation from the Wake

The sketch above raises three questions. (a) At what depth does the sharing begin? (b) How does cos²(θ/2) emerge? (c) Why doesn't every photon in the universe share a medium with every other? The framework answers all three from existing structure.

Part (a): Depth of Sharing

Every ⊙ exists inside a larger ⊙ (A2). When two photons are "entangled," what this means physically is that they were born from the same parent ⊙. In the standard experimental setup: a pump photon enters a nonlinear crystal and produces two daughter photons (signal and idler) via spontaneous parametric down-conversion. In framework terms, the pump photon is a ⊙ at scale λ₀; the crystal provides the boundary condition (○) that splits it into two daughter ⊙s at scale λ₁.

The depth of sharing is one scale above the daughters: the parent ⊙ from which both emerged. The parent's medium (the sub-photon ⊙s that composed the parent's wake) does not vanish when the parent splits. It persists as the shared medium connecting the two daughters. The daughters are two convergence points (two •s) moving apart through the same deeper field; their wakes ripple through the same sub-photon ⊙s.

daughter A⊙A: signal photon, moving left with its own inner Φ

Standard QM comparison. In QM, entanglement is a property of the joint state vector: |ψ⟩ = (|HV⟩ − |VH⟩)/√2. The "sharing" is encoded in the non-separability of this state; it cannot be written as a product |ψA⟩ ⊗ |ψB⟩. The framework's "shared medium at depth" plays the same structural role: it is the physical reason the state is non-separable. The daughters are not independent because the medium connecting them is one thing, not two.

Part (b): Deriving cos²(θ/2)

The framework's transmission law (§10.1.1, see 2.5D: Infolding) gives the energy transmitted between two scales:

T = cos²(Δφ / 2)

where Δφ is the phase mismatch between the transmitting and receiving apertures. This is derived from linearity, isotropy, conservation, and the complex structure of i. It governs every inter-scale transmission in the framework: force coupling, perception, signal fidelity. Now apply it to entangled measurement.

Setup

Two entangled photons share a parent ⊙ with a definite phase state. Conservation requires the daughters to carry complementary phases: if daughter A has phase φ, daughter B has phase φ + π (opposite polarization in the standard photon picture). The parent's phase φ is carried by the shared medium at depth.

Now place a measurement boundary (a polarizer, ○) on each path. Polarizer A is set to angle a. Polarizer B is set to angle b.

The Non-Local Step

In a local hidden-variable model, you would say: daughter A has a pre-existing polarization, daughter B has the complementary polarization, and each encounters its polarizer independently. You then average over the hidden phase φ. Bell proved this gives correlations bounded by |S| ≤ 2 (the CHSH inequality). Quantum mechanics predicts |S| = 2√2 and experiments confirm it. So the local model fails.

The framework's move: the shared medium at depth is not a classical hidden variable. It is a living field (sub-photon ⊙s still pumping). When measurement A fires (boundary ○A filters at angle a), this constrains the shared medium, because the medium connects to both daughters. The constraint is not a signal traveling from A to B; it is a joint filtering of one shared field by two boundaries simultaneously.

Standard QM comparison. In QM, the analogous move is "wave function collapse is instantaneous and non-local." When you measure photon A, the joint state updates globally. In Bohm's pilot wave, the quantum potential is explicitly non-local. The framework's version: the shared medium is one thing (A0: E = 1; there is only one energy), and filtering it at two points constrains it as a whole, not piecewise.

Applying T = cos²(Δφ/2) to the Joint System

The key: T = cos²(Δφ/2) applies not to each photon's individual encounter with its polarizer, but to the joint transmission of correlated phase through the pair of boundaries.

The relevant Δφ is the mismatch between the two measurement boundaries: Δφ = a − b = θ. This is the angle between the two polarizer settings. The shared medium carries a definite phase relationship (complementary); what varies between experiments is the relative orientation of the two filters that sample it.

The probability that both detectors agree (both transmit or both block) is:

P(agree) = cos²(θ / 2)

The probability that they disagree is:

P(disagree) = sin²(θ / 2)

The correlation function is:

E(a, b) = P(agree) − P(disagree) = cos²(θ/2) − sin²(θ/2) = cos(θ)
Standard QM comparison. For a polarization-entangled Bell state |Φ⁺⟩ = (|HH⟩ + |VV⟩)/√2, the quantum prediction is E(a, b) = cos(2θ) when using the Malus's law convention, or E(a, b) = −cos(θ) for the singlet state of spin-½ particles. The exact form depends on whether we use polarization angles (factor of 2) or spin projection angles (no factor). The framework's T = cos²(Δφ/2) matches the spin-½ singlet correlation directly. For polarization photons, the mapping is θspin = 2θpol, and the framework accommodates this because polarization is a 2D (Φ) quantity (two full rotations of polarization per one rotation of phase).

Why This Violates Bell's Inequality

Bell's inequality (CHSH form) says that for any local hidden-variable model:

|S| = |E(a,b) − E(a,b') + E(a',b) + E(a',b')| ≤ 2

Choose the standard optimal angles: a = 0, a' = π/2, b = π/4, b' = 3π/4. Then:

E(0, π/4) = cos(π/4) = 1/√2
E(0, 3π/4) = cos(3π/4) = −1/√2
E(π/2, π/4) = cos(π/4) = 1/√2
E(π/2, 3π/4) = cos(π/4) = 1/√2

|S| = |1/√2 − (−1/√2) + 1/√2 + 1/√2| = |4/√2| = 2√2 ≈ 2.828

This exceeds 2. The framework's correlation, derived from T = cos²(Δφ/2) applied to the shared medium, violates Bell's inequality by the same margin as quantum mechanics: 2√2, the Tsirelson bound.

Why the local model fails and this doesn't. A local hidden-variable model assumes each photon's outcome is determined by its own local state (the hidden variable φ) plus its own local measurement setting. The framework says: the medium is shared, so the two boundaries constrain one field jointly. The cos²(θ/2) is not the product of two independent local transmissions; it is the transmission through the angular relationship between two boundaries acting on the same underlying phase. One field, two filters, one joint transmission. That is why it exceeds the local bound.

Part (c): Why Not Universal Entanglement?

If the 1 is everywhere (A0) and all ⊙s are fractals of it (A2), why isn't every photon entangled with every other? Why do we see specific entangled pairs rather than a universal blur?

The answer is transmission loss across scale transitions. The same formula that produces the Bell correlation also explains why most photons are uncorrelated.

Two photons from the same SPDC event share a parent one scale above. The shared medium is one scale transition deep. The transmission through one scale boundary is:

T₁ = cos²(Δφ₁ / 2)

For the entangled pair, Δφ₁ is controlled by the parent's coherent phase; it is near zero within the pair. So T₁ ≈ 1: strong correlation.

Now consider two random photons from different sources. They share a common ancestor ⊙, but only at some much deeper scale (perhaps the universe itself). Between their scale and that ancestor, there are n intermediate scale transitions. Each transition has its own phase mismatch Δφₖ (random, since the intermediate ⊙s are unrelated). The total transmission compounds:

Ttotal = ∏k=1..n cos²(Δφₖ / 2)

For random phase mismatches, the expected value of each factor is ⟨cos²(Δφ/2)⟩ = 1/2 (the average of cos² over a full cycle). So:

⟨Ttotal⟩ = (1/2)n

Each scale transition halves the correlation. Two photons separated by 10 scale transitions have ⟨T⟩ ≈ 1/1024; by 40 scale transitions, ⟨T⟩ ≈ 10⁻¹². The correlation decays exponentially with scale distance. Not because the 1 stops being the 1, but because random phase at each intermediate boundary scrambles the coherence.

Standard QM comparison. In QM, decoherence plays this role. An entangled pair interacting with the environment (many uncontrolled degrees of freedom) rapidly loses its quantum correlations. The framework's version: each unrelated ⊙ between the pair is a random phase boundary, and transmission through random boundaries decays exponentially. Decoherence IS random-phase compounding across scale boundaries.

Back to the Chip

This interpretation reframes what a photonic chip is. A silicon or glass waveguide is not an empty tube through which photon-balls travel; it is a region of Φ shaped so the wake propagates cleanly. Low-loss materials are materials that do not disturb the wake. Nonlinearities are places where the wake is forced to interact with other wakes through real fold (matter). Phase stability is wake-phase stability.

Quantum photonic circuits work because glass is a substrate that lets the wake carry phase without breaking it. The reason glass is the right material for quantum photonics is the same as the reason photons barely fold: minimal disturbance of Φ on both sides of the interaction.

Summary

The photon is a whole ⊙: aperture (•), inner field (Φ), boundary (○). It moves through a never-empty medium made of smaller ⊙s. Those smaller circumpuncts respond to its passage, propagating phase disturbance: the wake. The photon's inner field stays internal; the wake is external, in the medium. Interference is the wake's pattern. Entanglement is two photon-cores sharing a deeper medium-identity. No splitting of photons; no collapse of cores; only the ordinary composition of a whole from its three constraints, moving through a medium that is itself composed of wholes at smaller scales.

The picture is old (de Broglie, Bohm, Couder) and the framework is new, but they meet cleanly because the framework already committed to the two premises the picture needs: no empty space, no smallest particle.
