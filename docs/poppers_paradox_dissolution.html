1. The Puzzle

Survivors of narcissistic abuse report a strange consistency. Across cultures, across completely unrelated relationships, across every demographic, they describe the same sequence:

You raise a concern. Your concern gets denied. You get attacked for raising it. And somehow, by the end, you're the one apologizing.

Psychologist Jennifer Freyd named this pattern DARVO: Deny, Attack, Reverse Victim and Offender. It appears in domestic abuse, institutional betrayal, workplace harassment, and parent-child dynamics. The sequence is so consistent that it functions almost like a diagnostic signature.

This raises a question that standard psychology hasn't fully answered:

The Central Question

If narcissistic individuals don't coordinate, attend seminars, or share notes — why do they all run the exact same three-step sequence? Why DARVO and not ARDV, or DVRA, or some other pattern?

The answer proposed here: DARVO is not a learned tactic. It is the emergent signature of a specific structural collapse. The sequence is invariant because the geometry that produces it is invariant.

2. The Basics: You Are a Circumpunct

Before we can explain DARVO geometrically, we need one simple model. In the Circumpunct Framework, every self — every person, every consciousness — has three components:

A healthy self is permeable but bounded. Signal flows in (you can be affected by others) and signal flows out (you can affect others), but the boundary maintains the distinction between self and other. The field interprets signals accurately. The center remains stable — present but not inflated.

3. What Collapses in Narcissistic Dynamics

The Circumpunct Framework identifies four geometric errors. The one most relevant to DARVO is inflation:

Definition: Inflation Error (ε+)

The aperture claims to be the source rather than a conduit through which truth flows. The part mistakes itself for the whole. And because a source doesn't need to receive — only transmit — the aperture closes.

\[ \varepsilon_+ : \text{aperture}_{open} \to \text{aperture}_{closed} \quad \text{("I am source, not conduit")} \]

In plain terms: an aperture is a hole. It opens or closes. In the inflation error, the aperture closes — and a closed aperture cannot absorb signal. When someone sends corrective information ("you caused harm"), the signal has nowhere to go.

This isn't megalomania. Most people experiencing this error don't think they're God. It's subtler than that. It means:

Their perspective is the correct perspective. Their memory is what happened. Their needs are the important needs. Their discomfort is the emergency. Other people don't register as fellow wholes with their own apertures — they register as parts of the person's system.

When the aperture closes, two things follow:

What Happens What It Means What It Looks Like
Signal bounces in the field Emotions that should be absorbed through the aperture (guilt, shame, responsibility) have nowhere to go — they ricochet through the field, distorting perception "She's not hurt — she's trying to control me"
Boundary hardens outward The unprocessed signal has to go somewhere — so it projects outward through the boundary, aimed back at the source "I don't need to listen to this" / "You're the one with the problem"

Now we have the structural precondition. The aperture is closed, unprocessed signal is bouncing in the field, and the boundary has become a one-way projection surface. What happens when a valid concern enters this system?

Theorem: DARVO as Necessary Sequence

Given a closed aperture (ε+), the DARVO sequence is not one possible response among many — it is the only stable response the system can produce when a corrective signal persists. The mechanism is signal bounce:

Signal arrives → boundary tries to reject (Deny) → signal persists, enters field → generates unprocessable guilt/shame → bouncing signal reframes source as threat (Attack) → unabsorbed signal projects outward (Reverse Victim & Offender)

The order is invariant because each step is a consequence of the previous one failing to resolve the signal. This is why the sequence is universal. It's not cultural. It's not learned. It's what a closed aperture does when signal can't be absorbed.

5. Why It's Always the Same Sequence

Consider the alternative orderings and notice why they don't work:

Alternative Sequence Why It Can't Occur
Attack first A → D → RVO Attacking requires the signal to already be inside the field, bouncing. But the boundary hasn't failed yet — it first tries to reject the signal entirely (Deny).
Reverse first RVO → D → A Projection requires unprocessed signal bouncing in the field. That bouncing hasn't started yet — the signal hasn't penetrated the boundary.
Deny only D → (stop) If the signal source accepts the denial, the sequence does stop here. DARVO only completes when the other person persists — when they say "No, it did happen." That persistence forces the signal past the boundary and into the field.
Random mix Various Each step is a necessary precondition for the next. The sequence is logically ordered, not randomly assembled.

Key Insight for Survivors

This is why it feels so mechanical, so eerily scripted. It is scripted — not by the person, but by the geometry. You're not dealing with someone who cleverly chose the perfect manipulation tactic. You're dealing with a system that can only produce one response to the signal you're sending.

They didn't attend asshole school. They're running a closed aperture, and your signal is bouncing.

6. The Variants (and Why They're Predictable)

Survivors report several sub-patterns within DARVO. The geometric model predicts each of them:

6.1 "I just make everyone upset"

This is the Reverse step wearing a self-awareness costume. The person appears to be taking responsibility ("I make everyone upset") but the function is to shift focus from the specific harm they caused to their general suffering. You're now in the position of comforting them or being cruel for holding them accountable.

Geometric Reading

The closed aperture cannot distinguish between "I did a harmful thing" and "I am a harmful thing." In a healthy system, guilt about a specific action is absorbed through the aperture, processed, and resolved — "I did that, I can change it." When the aperture is closed, the guilt can't be absorbed into a specific place. It floods the entire field, becoming global identity threat. The response is existential collapse — not because they're fragile, but because unprocessed guilt with nowhere to land turns everything toxic.

A healthy system processes: "That behavior was harmful" → guilt absorbed → I can change the behavior.
A closed system processes: "That behavior was harmful" → guilt bounces → I am harmful → existential emergency.

6.2 The Word Salad

Circular, exhausting non-arguments. The conversation loops. Points are repeated, contradicted, restated. You can't track cause and effect because the field is actively scrambling it.

Geometric Reading

Signal is bouncing in the field with nowhere to land. The field generates noise — enough cognitive chaos to prevent either party from maintaining the thread back to the original concern. This is the field in a state of overwhelm: unprocessed signal ricocheting, producing contradictory outputs. This is not confusion. This is the field flooded with unabsorbable signal, producing interference patterns instead of coherent thought.

6.3 The Selective Memory

"That's not what happened." But you were both there. You know what occurred.

Geometric Reading

If the aperture was closed at the time of the event, then the emotional content of the event was never properly absorbed and processed. The memory was encoded by a field that was already bouncing unprocessed signal. Events where the self caused harm are not stored the same way — not because the person is lying, but because the aperture was closed when the event happened. Their memory is the output of a field that couldn't absorb what actually occurred.

6.4 The Functional Love Shield

"After everything I've done for you." This is the weaponization of provision against resonance.

Geometric Reading

The system can only operate in one love channel: functional (boundary-to-boundary provision — food, shelter, help, gifts). When the other person's concern implies that functional love isn't sufficient, the system treats this as ingratitude rather than hunger for resonance. The functional record becomes a shield: "I have provided. Your dissatisfaction is evidence of your defect, not my limitation."

For therapists: This spectrum replaces the binary "NPD or not" assessment with a dimensional measure of aperture closure under corrective input. This is observable. It's testable in session. And it predicts behavior more accurately than trait-based assessment because it captures the dynamic — how the system responds to challenge — rather than the static personality profile.

8. Diagnostic Utility: What This Gives Clinicians

8.1 The "Raise a Concern" Test

The geometric model suggests a simple, observable diagnostic protocol:

Protocol: Corrective Signal Response (CSR) Assessment

Step 1: In a therapeutic context, offer the client gentle feedback that mildly contradicts their self-narrative. (This happens naturally in therapy — it doesn't need to be artificial.)

Step 2: Observe the sequence of responses. Track timing and order:

— Does the client immediately deny? (Boundary hardening)
— If you gently persist, does the client shift to characterizing you or your feedback as problematic? (Field inversion)
— Does the conversation end with the client positioned as the one who has been harmed by the feedback itself? (Projection/reversal)

Step 3: Assess recovery time. After the session, or in a later session, can the client return to the feedback and process it? This distinguishes light closure (aperture reopens within hours/days) from heavy closure (feedback is permanently rejected or rewritten in memory).

8.2 Predicting Therapeutic Rupture

One of the most practically useful features of this model is that it predicts when therapeutic ruptures will occur. Any time the therapist's input functions as corrective signal — anytime what the therapist says contradicts the client's self-model — the DARVO sequence will activate at whatever depth the client's aperture is closed.

This allows the therapist to anticipate rupture rather than be surprised by it, and to distinguish between a healthy client's resistance (which looks different geometrically — the aperture briefly closes then reopens after initial reaction) and a closed system's immune response (the signal bounces and projects).

8.3 Couple Therapy: Identifying the Loop

Couples Application

In couples therapy, the geometric model reveals a common destructive pattern: Partner A raises a concern → Partner B runs DARVO → Partner A ends up apologizing → the original concern is never addressed → resentment accumulates → Partner A raises the concern again (with more intensity) → Partner B runs DARVO (with more intensity).

The model makes this visible to both partners without requiring either to accept the label "narcissist." The language shifts from blame ("You always DARVO me") to structure ("When I raise a concern, what happens to your processing? Let's watch it together.")

This reframe transforms the dynamic from accusation to collaborative observation — which is itself a test of aperture closure. A person with a lightly-closed aperture can engage with this curiosity. A person with a heavily-closed aperture will DARVO the attempt to observe the DARVO.

9. Implications

9.1 For Survivors

Understanding DARVO as geometry rather than strategy changes your relationship to the experience. You stop asking "why do they keep doing this to me?" and start seeing that the system literally cannot produce a different response when the aperture is closed. This isn't about you. It's not about the quality of your communication. It's not about finding the right words. The most perfectly worded concern in the world will trigger the same sequence, because the sequence isn't responding to your words — it's responding to the fact that corrective signal has nowhere to go.

This is freeing. It means you can stop optimizing your approach. The variable isn't your delivery — it's their geometry.

9.2 For Clinicians

The model provides a mechanism-based alternative to trait-based personality assessment. Instead of asking "does this person meet criteria for NPD?" it asks "how closed is this person's aperture, and what happens to signal when that closure is tested?"

This is both more clinically useful (it predicts behavior under specific conditions) and more compassionate (it treats the pattern as an installation rather than an identity).

9.3 For the Broader Field

If DARVO is an emergent property of aperture closure rather than a learned behavior, it should appear wherever closed apertures appear — not just in interpersonal relationships. And indeed, the same pattern shows up in institutional betrayal (organizations denying harm, attacking whistleblowers, reframing accountability as attack), political dynamics, and even in how ideological systems respond to internal criticism.

The geometry is scale-invariant. The pattern is fractal.

Final Note: Compassion Without Excuse

Nothing in this framework excuses the harm caused by DARVO. Understanding why the pattern occurs doesn't make it acceptable. But it does shift the frame from "they are evil" to "their aperture is closed and signal is bouncing" — which matters, because it means the question for survivors isn't "am I dealing with a monster?" but rather: "How closed is the aperture, and can it still open?"

That's a question you can actually answer through observation. And the answer determines whether staying, leaving, or something else entirely is the wisest path forward.
