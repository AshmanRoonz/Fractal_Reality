1. The ladder, sounded

Eight stations, one octave: do re mi fa sol la ti do′. The point the ear can settle that the eye keeps sliding off: the tonic is shared. Do′ does not end the scale and then a new scale begins. Do′ is the next scale's do. One event, not two. So two octaves are fifteen notes, not sixteen.

The tuning toggle is a real question, not a garnish. The corpus labels the stations do-re-mi-fa-sol-la-ti-do′, which is the diatonic scale: unequal steps (tone, tone, semitone, tone, tone, tone, semitone). But the ladder's own coordinates are equally spaced: 0, 0.5, 1, 1.5, 2, 2.5, 3, 3.5. Equal spacing with a shared tonic gives seven equal divisions of the octave, which is a real tuning system and also exactly the ℤ₇ residue count, R = 7. Diatonic is what you wrote. 7-equal is what the arithmetic says. They do not sound the same. Your call which one the framework actually means; the solfège may be a naming convention rather than a claim about intervals.

2. Shepard tone vs. a real octave

The Inflation Lie has a sound. A Shepard tone rises forever and arrives nowhere: chroma cycles while height never moves. That is performed growth. A true octave gains height. Play them side by side and watch the counters; the ears say both are climbing, and only one of them is.

Both counters advance the same chroma, one full turn per cycle. Only the true octave advances height. The Shepard is built from eight octave-spaced partials under a fixed Gaussian window in log-frequency: as every partial glides up, the top one fades out exactly as a new one fades in at the bottom, so after one full cycle the sound is bit-identical to where it started. That is not a trick of attention. There is genuinely no ascent to hear. Let it run a minute and the counter will still read 0.00.

3. The helix

Two coordinates per station: chroma (which note; the angle) and height (how high; the rise). A circle would lose the height. A line would lose the return. The axis is a helix, and the tonics stack on one vertical, because the tonic is the one place adjacent octaves touch. Drag to rotate. Click the helix to sound the nearest station.
