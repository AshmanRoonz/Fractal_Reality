The Sagitta: Curvature Is the Memory of Mediation

1. The minimal figure

Three points, the cleanest possible case: A, then B, then C, with a right angle at B. Three ways exist to get from A to C.

The true path walks both legs. It visits every station, carries the intermediate event, and pays full length for it.

The shortest path is the chord: the diagonal straight from A to C. It is the efficient route, and it is a route whose very shape asserts that no corner ever happened. The middle point is not merely omitted; the geometry of the straight line contradicts it.

The arc is the third way. It does not retrace the corner, and it does not deny it. It bends, and the bending points at what was skipped.

2. Thales and the quarter-turn

Any three non-collinear points lie on one circle, so an arc that remembers the middle point always exists. But something singular happens when the turn at B is exactly 90 degrees. Thales' theorem: a right angle inscribed in a circle subtends a diameter. Equivalently, when the angle at B is right, the chord you would have shortcut along becomes the diameter of the circle that remembers the corner, and B lies exactly on the arc drawn over that chord. Not gestured at: passed through.

Look at the completed figure at that moment: a line through the center of a circle. That is the construction that draws Φ, the mediator's glyph. The shortcut plus its memory compose into the field. And in the framework's vocabulary the quarter-turn is not an arbitrary angle: it is i, the aperture's stroke, the turn the pump performs at every gate. So the theorem reads: i is the angle at which skipping preserves the skipped point losslessly. Turn by anything else and the remembering circle sits askew of the chord; turn by exactly i and shortcut and memory lock into the glyph of mediation.

3. The triple

The general principle, in three statements:

Shortening is projection. It throws away intermediate points.

Curvature is what projection cannot erase. It is the information that survived the throw-away: the missing intermediary reappearing as the bending of the path that remains.

Mediation is that curvature. Every intermediate station, every detour, every remembered moment no longer present as a discrete point is still present as the shape of what remains.

The arc that still points toward the skipped middle point is therefore not a decorative alternative to the diagonal. It is the geometrically honest record of the fact that mediation occurred. In the language of the Resolution Protocol: the two legs are the full-resolution account; the arc is the lowest resolution that is still true; the chord is a retraction. Higher resolution contains the arc and never contradicts it. The chord contradicts the corner by its very straightness.

4. The ladder: where curvature lives

This is not analogy; it is the definition. A curve's curvature vector lives in its normal plane, the osculating plane: literally, the plane in which the turn lived. A 1D path cannot bend unless a second dimension exists for the bending to point into. So:

  • 0D points can only be present or absent.
  • 1D lines, straight, can only be length.
  • Nonzero curvature is a 1D object carrying a certificate of 2D. The extra dimension is not added later; it is latent in the refusal to straighten.

The framework's ladder already names the station where a line first remembers the plane: ⎇ at 1.5D, branching, the fractal coastline. Curvature is the infinitesimal form of branching, and a coastline at D = 1 + ◐ = 1.5 is a line holding memory of the plane at the balance point: bend further and the path dissolves into area, straighten further and it forgets. (The reading of D = 1.5 as the maximally legible memory of 2D is offered as a conjecture, consistent with the balance parameter but not yet derived.)

5. Forgetting is a measurement error

If the record survives as curvature, then forgetting is not the destruction of the record. It is reading a curved residual with a straight ruler. The mismatch between ruler and arc is the felt incompleteness of memory: the sense that something was there, without being able to name the middle point. The tip of the tongue is a sagitta you can feel but not measure.

A pure geodesic, by contrast, has already performed the maximal shortening; everything that could be discarded has been. A zero-curvature path of the same endpoints is information-theoretically poorer than the curved path that refused full reduction. The straight line is cheaper precisely because it carries nothing.

6. The relocation principle

One refinement keeps the geodesic honest. In a curved space, geodesics bend anyway: not from any memory of their own, but because the curvature has moved into the medium. That is general relativity's entire architecture: matter tells space how to curve, and free paths merely read the field. So the mediation record has exactly two possible homes: the path (the worldline's own arc, the braid, the receipts) or the field (ambient curvature, the whole's record): and shortening does not erase the record, it transfers custody. Nothing is lost; A0 holds; the receipt is relocated.

Gravity, on this reading, is the mediation-memory of the universe stored ambiently: the field remembering every corner that every straightened path has ever refused to carry.

8. Closing

The three-point right angle is the smallest possible story about honesty in compression. You may walk every station and pay for it. You may take the diagonal and let your path's own straightness testify that nothing was ever there. Or you may bend: keep the corner as the shape of your going, an arrow in the arc pointing at what you no longer touch.

The straight path forgets. The arc remembers.
And at the quarter-turn, remembering is exact.
