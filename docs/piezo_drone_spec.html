Piezo Disc Drone Concept Specification & Development Path · v0.1 · March 26, 2026

A disc-shaped aircraft with no propellers, no motors, and no conventional moving parts. The entire surface is piezoelectric skin that undulates like a ray, creating travelling waves that organize the boundary layer air into cymatic channels. Thrust is generated by the skin itself. Phase controls direction. Amplitude controls magnitude. The disc flies by vibrating.

◎

§01 Vision

Every existing aircraft moves air with a separate mechanism: propellers, jet engines, rotors. The air is passive; the machine does all the work. This is inefficient, noisy, and mechanically complex.

A piezo disc drone inverts this. The entire body surface vibrates at controlled frequencies, creating travelling wave patterns in the air at the boundary layer. The air self-organizes into flow channels (cymatics) that produce net downward momentum. The aircraft does not push the air; it gives the air a pattern to follow, and the pattern does the work.

Core Concept
No propellers.         The skin IS the engine.
No motors.             Piezoelectric film IS the actuator.
No rotating parts.     Travelling waves ARE the mechanism.

Input:    AC electrical signal (frequency, phase, amplitude)
Process:  Piezo skin vibrates → travelling waves on surface
          → cymatic channels form in boundary layer
          → organized airflow produces net thrust
Output:   Silent, omnidirectional flight

Why a Disc?

Radial symmetry. A disc has no preferred direction; every radial axis is equivalent. This means thrust direction is entirely determined by the wave pattern, not the body shape. Propagate waves from center to edge: vertical thrust (hover). Propagate waves from left to right: lateral translation. Tilt the wave propagation angle: pitch and roll. The same surface produces any thrust vector by changing the electrical phase pattern across zones.

Maximum surface area to volume ratio. For a given mass, a thin disc has more skin area than any other compact shape. More skin = more piezo actuator area = more thrust-generating surface.

The disc is also the shape of a vibrating membrane (drum), whose resonant modes are among the most thoroughly studied in physics (Bessel functions, Chladni patterns on circular plates). The eigenmode structure is known exactly.

Biological Analog

The manta ray. Flat disc-like body. No propeller equivalent. Undulating fin margins create travelling waves that generate both lift and thrust. The ray's body is the engine. The piezo disc drone is a synthetic ray that flies in air instead of swimming in water.

§02 How It Flies

The Travelling Wave Mechanism

When a flexible surface undulates with a travelling wave, it creates an asymmetric pressure distribution in the adjacent fluid. On the side where the surface is moving into the fluid (compression), pressure increases. On the side where the surface is pulling away (rarefaction), pressure decreases. Because the wave travels, these pressure zones move across the surface in sequence, creating a net momentum flux in the fluid.

For a horizontal disc, a radially outward travelling wave (center to edge) pushes air downward and outward beneath the disc. The reaction force is upward: lift. The mechanism is the same one a ray uses: undulatory fin propulsion via added-mass thrust generation.

§03 Biological Precedent

This is not a new propulsion concept. It is the oldest one. Undulatory locomotion predates every other form of animal movement. Before legs, before wings, before jet propulsion, organisms moved by sending waves through their bodies.

The Ray Model

Manta rays and eagle rays use rajiform locomotion: travelling waves propagate from anterior to posterior along wide, flat pectoral fins. The fins have no rigid skeletal elements at the margins; instead, a three-dimensional muscular hydrostat (a matrix of muscle fibers oriented in multiple directions) generates the wave through sequential contraction. Thrust is produced by the added-mass mechanism: each wave crest accelerates a volume of water downward and backward, and the reaction pushes the ray forward and up.

Key properties of ray locomotion relevant to the disc drone:

Design Principle

Biology solved the "no moving parts propulsion" problem half a billion years ago. The ray has no propeller, no rotor, no jet. Its body is the engine. The disc drone copies the principle, replaces muscle with piezo, and moves from water to air. The physics is identical; only the medium's density changes.

§04 Cymatics of Flight

The disc surface is a Chladni plate. When driven at specific frequencies, it forms standing wave patterns with well-defined nodal lines and antinodes. For a circular disc, these patterns are described by Bessel functions and characterized by two numbers: the number of radial nodes and the number of diametral nodes.

Standing waves alone do not produce net thrust (symmetric pressure above and below cancels). Thrust requires travelling waves, which break the symmetry. A travelling wave on a disc is created by superposing two standing wave modes with a 90° phase offset (the same trick that creates a rotating magnetic field from two coils in quadrature).

From Standing Wave to Travelling Wave
Standing wave (no net thrust):
  Mode A:  cos(nθ) · cos(ωt)         ← symmetric, oscillates in place

Standing wave (orthogonal, 90° spatial offset):
  Mode B:  sin(nθ) · cos(ωt)         ← also symmetric

Combine with 90° TEMPORAL offset:
  A + B':  cos(nθ)·cos(ωt) + sin(nθ)·sin(ωt) = cos(nθ - ωt)

  This is a TRAVELLING WAVE rotating around the disc at angular velocity ω/n.

  It has a preferred direction. It breaks symmetry. It produces thrust.

By controlling which standing wave modes are excited and what phase offsets they carry, the disc can produce travelling waves in any direction, at any speed, with any spatial pattern. The cymatic channels that form in the boundary layer air follow the travelling wave, organizing the airflow into structured vortex streets that produce net momentum flux.

Frequency Selection

Different frequencies excite different cymatic modes on the disc. Lower frequencies produce patterns with few, large features (wide channels, gentle undulations). Higher frequencies produce patterns with many fine features (narrow channels, rapid oscillations). The optimal frequency for thrust generation depends on the relationship between wavelength and boundary layer thickness.

The disc can be driven at multiple frequencies simultaneously (chord driving; see §07 for the four-voice architecture that the lenticular geometry enables). Low-frequency bending modes handle bulk air movement (long wavelength, deep penetration into the air column). High-frequency membrane modes handle fine boundary layer organization (short wavelength, close to the surface). Together they form a chord: each voice organizes a different scale of the airflow simultaneously, from the centimeter-scale undulation that entrains bulk air down to the sub-millimeter acoustic streaming that smooths the boundary layer. This is the harmonic cascade from the circumpunct device, applied to aerodynamics.

§05 Disc Modes

Chladni Modes of a Circular Plate

A circular plate clamped (or free) at the center has resonant modes characterized by (m, n) where m = number of diametral node lines and n = number of circular (radial) node lines. The fundamental (0,0) is uniform flexure; (1,0) has one diameter splitting the disc into two halves vibrating out of phase; (0,1) has one concentric ring node.

Key Disc Modes and Their Flight Applications
Mode (m,n)PatternTravelling WaveFlight Application
(0,0) Whole disc flexes uniformly (breathing) Pulsation: disc pumps air down/up like a jellyfish bell Vertical thrust (hover assist)
(1,0) One diametral line; disc rocks like a seesaw Rotating wave: disc tilts continuously around its center Pitch / roll control
(2,0) Two diametral lines; disc flexes into saddle shape Rotating saddle wave (like stirring coffee) Rotational thrust (yaw)
(0,1) One concentric ring; center and edge vibrate out of phase Radial travelling wave: ripple from center to edge Primary hover mode (radial pump)
(0,2) Two concentric rings Radial travelling wave with shorter wavelength Higher-efficiency hover (finer undulation)
(m,n) combined Complex patterns with both radial and diametral nodes Spiral travelling waves Combined thrust + rotation

Superposition for Full Control

HOVER:   (0,1) radial travelling wave, symmetric
         All edges pump air downward equally.
         Thrust: vertical.

TRANSLATE: (0,1) + (1,0) superposition
           Radial wave biased toward one side.
           Stronger pump on trailing edge → net lateral thrust.

PITCH:   (1,0) travelling wave
         Disc rocks; leading edge rises, trailing edge dips.
         Asymmetric pressure → pitching moment.

YAW:     (2,0) rotating wave
         Saddle rotates around center.
         Tangential air momentum → torque around vertical axis.

CLIMB:   (0,1) at higher amplitude
         Same pattern, more displacement → more thrust.

DESCEND: (0,1) at lower amplitude, or reverse wave direction
         Reduce pump rate, or pump air upward.

Every flight maneuver maps to a specific combination of disc modes, phases, and amplitudes. The flight controller's job is to decompose the desired thrust vector into the correct superposition of modes and drive the piezo zones accordingly.

§07 Disc Structure

Why Lenticular (Convex Both Sides)

The disc is not flat. It is lenticular: convex on top, convex on bottom, like a lens or a lentil. This shape is critical for three reasons.

First, chord driving. A flat plate has one family of vibration modes (bending). A curved shell has two mechanically independent families: bending modes (surface flexes in and out) and membrane modes (surface stretches and compresses in-plane). On a flat plate these couple together. On a curved shell they separate, because curvature links in-plane stress to out-of-plane displacement. This separation gives you two independent frequency channels on the same surface: you can drive bending modes at one frequency and membrane modes at another simultaneously without interference. Two voices from one skin.

With piezo on both the top and bottom convex surfaces, each surface contributes its own pair of mode families. That gives four independent voices:

Four-Voice Chord Architecture
VoiceSurfaceMode TypeFrequency RangeAerodynamic Role
Bass Bottom shell Bending (out-of-plane) Low (50-500 Hz) Bulk air entrainment: large undulations push thick layer of air downward. Primary thrust. Long wavelength = deep penetration into air below disc.
Tenor Bottom shell Membrane (in-plane) Mid (500 Hz - 10 kHz) Boundary layer organization: in-plane vibrations create cymatic channels in the air at the bottom surface. Reduces turbulence. Structures the airflow that the bass voice is pushing.
Alto Top shell Bending (out-of-plane) Low-mid (100 Hz - 2 kHz) Low-pressure generation: undulations on top surface create periodic rarefaction zones above the disc. Pressure differential contributes to lift. Like the top surface of a wing, but active.
Treble Top shell Membrane (in-plane) High (1 kHz - 100 kHz) Fine-scale acoustic streaming: high-frequency in-plane vibrations create SAW-like momentum transfer in the air above. Boundary layer thinning. Drag reduction.

All four voices play simultaneously. The bass moves bulk air (thrust). The tenor structures that airflow into efficient channels (drag reduction on the thrust side). The alto creates low-pressure suction above (additional lift). The treble smooths the upper boundary layer (drag reduction on the suction side). Each voice operates at a different scale, organizing a different depth of the airflow. Together they are a chord: multiple frequencies, each handling a different layer of the aerodynamic problem.

Second, structural stiffness. A flat disc is floppy for its weight. A convex shell is dramatically stiffer (eggshell principle: curvature converts bending loads into membrane stress, which thin structures resist efficiently). The lenticular shape lets you build the disc thinner and lighter while maintaining enough rigidity at the center to support the electronics. Less mass = less thrust needed.

Third, higher fundamental frequency. Curvature raises the fundamental resonant frequency compared to a flat plate of the same diameter and thickness. A flat 30cm disc might resonate at 50-100 Hz (wavelength comparable to disc size; limited number of useful modes). A lenticular shell of the same diameter could have a fundamental at 200-500 Hz, pushing more of its harmonic series into the aerodynamically useful range. More usable modes = finer control over the wave patterns.

Flexibility Profile

The disc is not uniformly flexible. The center must be rigid enough to support the electronics and battery. The edges must be flexible enough to undulate with useful amplitude. The transition between rigid core and flexible margin determines the disc's modal structure. The curvature also varies: steeper near the center (stiffer), flattening toward the edge (more flexible).

The Chord Principle

A flat plate is a monophonic instrument: one vibration family, one voice. A lenticular shell is polyphonic: curvature separates bending and membrane modes into independent channels, top and bottom surfaces double it, and the curvature gradient spreads resonance across the radius. The disc can play a four-voice chord where each voice organizes a different scale of the airflow: bulk entrainment, channel formation, suction generation, and boundary layer smoothing. All simultaneously. All from one continuous piezo skin driven by a multi-frequency AC signal.

Amplification Strategies

PVDF's 0.1-0.2% strain is the core engineering challenge. For a 100mm long strip, 0.1% strain = 0.1mm displacement. This is adequate for ultrasonic acoustic streaming but marginal for macroscopic undulation. Strategies to amplify:

StrategyAmplificationMechanism
Bimorph bending10-50×Two piezo layers bonded to a substrate; differential strain curves the structure. Displacement at the tip of a cantilevered bimorph scales with length².
Resonant amplificationQ factor (10-100×)Drive at the disc's natural resonant frequency. Energy accumulates over cycles. Displacement = static displacement × Q.
Lever geometry2-10×Origami-inspired fold patterns convert small in-plane strain to large out-of-plane displacement (Harvard RoboBee method).
Stacked/laminatedN layersMultiple piezo layers in series increase total strain additively.

Combining bimorph bending with resonant amplification is the most promising path. A bimorph strip at resonance can achieve tip displacements of 0.5-5 mm from a 100V drive signal, which is in the useful range for undulatory thrust in air.

Key Insight

The disc does not need to flex by centimeters. A manta ray's fin undulates with amplitude roughly 10-20% of its chord length. For a 30cm disc with a 5cm flexible margin, that's 5-10mm of edge displacement. Bimorph piezo at resonance can achieve this. The displacement is small but the surface area is large, and thrust scales with area × displacement × frequency².

Dual-Use: Actuator and Sensor

Piezo film is bidirectional: apply voltage, it moves; move it, it generates voltage. The same skin that drives the undulation can also sense the aerodynamic response. Between drive pulses (or on dedicated sense zones), the piezo film measures the pressure fluctuations at the surface, giving the flight controller real-time feedback on the boundary layer state.

This closes the loop. The controller drives the skin, the skin modifies the airflow, the airflow presses back on the skin, the skin reports the pressure, and the controller adjusts. The disc has proprioception: it can feel the air.

⚠ The Thrust Gap

The required 14 N/m² is achievable by multi-stage EHD but above what simple piezo synthetic jets have demonstrated in air. Biological undulatory thrust is in the right range but operates in water (800× denser than air). This is the central engineering challenge. Possible paths to close the gap: resonant amplification (Q factor 10-100×), chord driving (multiple frequencies), optimized disc geometry, or hybrid approach (piezo undulation + EHD ionic boost).

    f ≈ 1 kHz (operating frequency)
    P = π × 1000 × 10e-9 × 100² ≈ 0.31 W per zone

  24 zones: ~7.5 W total piezo drive

  Electronics overhead: ~2 W (MCU, DAC, boost converter losses)

  Total estimated: ~10 W

  With 500mAh LiPo at 3.7V = 1.85 Wh
  Flight time (if thrust is sufficient): ~10-12 minutes

  With 1000mAh: ~3.7 Wh → ~20-25 minutes

  Note: Piezo is capacitive (reactive power), so much of the
  energy can be recovered each cycle with resonant drive circuits
  (inductor in series with piezo forms LC tank). Recovery could
  cut real power to 30-50% of the above estimate, extending
  flight time to 20-40 min on 1000mAh.

The power is surprisingly low because piezo actuators are capacitive, not resistive. They store and release energy each cycle rather than dissipating it. The main power cost is the electronic overhead, not the actuation itself. This is fundamentally different from motors and propellers, where most power goes directly into moving air.

Minimum Viable Experiment
Build a simple piezo bimorph disc (not full drone):

  - 4-8 electrode zones (not 24)

Cost: ~$50-100
Time: 1-2 weekends

Expand bench disc to multi-zone drive:
  - 8-12 zones with independent phase control
  - Sweep frequency, phase offset, amplitude
  - Map thrust vs parameters
  - Identify resonant modes that produce maximum thrust
  - Measure thrust directionality (can phase steer the force vector?)

Key question: what thrust density (N/m²) can we achieve?
If > 5 N/m²: proceed to Phase 2
If 1-5 N/m²: need amplification strategy or hybrid approach

  - 24 zones
  - Full flight controller (IMU + mode decomposition)

Test: can it reduce its apparent weight on a scale?
Test: can it control attitude (tilt when commanded)?
Test: can it yaw (rotate when commanded)?

Add battery. Remove tether. Fly.
This is the milestone.

§12 Experiments

Experiment 1: Single-Zone Thrust Test

Purpose: Measure thrust from a single piezo bimorph zone

Setup:
  - Piezo bimorph strip (PVDF on both sides of 0.5mm substrate)
  - Dimensions: 50mm × 20mm
  - Clamped at one end, free at other
  - Positioned above precision scale with small platform
  - Gap between bimorph tip and platform: ~2mm

Procedure:
  1. Drive bimorph at sweep of frequencies (100 Hz to 50 kHz)
  2. Record scale reading change at each frequency
  3. Identify resonant peaks
  4. At each resonant peak, measure thrust vs voltage (amplitude)

Expected:
  - Maximum thrust at bimorph resonant frequency
  - Thrust proportional to V² (piezo force scales with V²)
  - Measurable airflow below bimorph (synthetic jet effect)

Experiment 2: Travelling Wave on Disc

Purpose: Confirm travelling wave generation on a circular disc

Setup:
  - 15cm disc (acrylic or FR4, 1-2mm thick)
  - 4 piezo patches at 0°, 90°, 180°, 270°
  - Drive adjacent patches with 90° phase offset

Procedure:
  1. Drive all 4 patches at same frequency, 90° phase sequence
  2. Scatter fine powder (lycopodium, salt) on disc surface
  3. Observe: do particles transport in a consistent direction?
     (If yes: travelling wave confirmed; if they collect at nodes: standing wave only)
  4. Reverse phase sequence → particles should transport in opposite direction
  5. Photograph patterns at each frequency

This is a direct cymatic visualization of the disc's wave modes.

Experiment 3: Disc Thrust Map

Purpose: Map thrust vs frequency × phase × amplitude for full disc

Setup:
  - 15cm disc with 8-12 zones
  - Mounted on 3-axis force sensor or precision scale
  - Multi-channel waveform generator

Procedure:
  1. Radial travelling wave (center → edge):
     Sweep frequency, measure vertical thrust at each
  2. Rotating travelling wave (angular):
     Sweep frequency, measure torque at each
  3. Directional travelling wave (left → right):
     Sweep frequency, measure lateral force at each
  4. At best frequencies, sweep amplitude
  5. At best frequencies, sweep phase offset between zones

Output: 3D thrust maps. The flight controller will use these as lookup tables.

Experiment 4: Boundary Layer Visualization

Purpose: See the cymatic channels forming in the air

Setup:
  - Disc operating at best thrust frequency
  - Smoke wire or fog generator producing thin smoke sheet
  - Laser sheet illumination (green laser + cylindrical lens)
  - High-speed camera (even 240fps phone camera helps)

Procedure:
  1. Position smoke sheet parallel to disc surface, ~2-5mm gap
  2. Activate disc: observe smoke pattern
  3. Compare: disc off (random turbulence) vs disc on (organized channels)
  4. Photograph/video the cymatic flow structure

This is the money shot: visible proof that the disc organizes airflow.

§13 Open Problems

Critical Unknowns
#ProblemWhy It MattersPath to Answer
1 Thrust density in air Can undulatory thrust in air (density 1.2 kg/m³) match what works in water (density 1000 kg/m³)? The 800× density difference means 800× less reaction mass per unit volume. Experiment 1 and 3. If insufficient: increase frequency (more cycles per second = more air processed), increase disc area, or add EHD assist.
2 Resonant mode control Can 24 zones produce clean travelling waves, or do unwanted modes contaminate the pattern? Experiment 2. Modal analysis of disc structure. May need more zones or structural tuning (varying thickness profile).
3 Displacement amplification PVDF strain is 0.1-0.2%. Is bimorph bending at resonance enough, or do we need MFC or DEAP? Experiment 1. Measure actual displacement with laser vibrometer. If insufficient with PVDF, test MFC (10× higher d31).
4 Aerodynamic coupling Does the air load damp the disc vibration enough to kill thrust? (Radiation impedance mismatch between solid disc and air.) Compare disc resonance in vacuum vs air. If Q drops dramatically in air, the air is absorbing the energy (good for thrust; bad for resonant amplification).
5 Control bandwidth Can the flight controller update the mode decomposition fast enough for stable flight? Disc dynamics may require >1 kHz update rate. Simulation first; Phase 2 tethered test.
6 Scaling laws Does thrust per unit area increase or decrease with disc size? Biological undulation suggests larger is better (higher Reynolds number), but structural resonance frequencies drop with size. Test at multiple disc diameters: 10cm, 20cm, 30cm, 50cm.

The Hybrid Option

If pure piezo undulatory thrust proves insufficient for hover in air, a hybrid approach combines two no-moving-parts technologies:

PIEZO UNDULATION (primary)
  + provides lift via travelling wave thrust
  + provides all attitude control via phase/amplitude
  + provides boundary layer drag reduction

EHD IONIC WIND (boost)
  + thin wire emitters along disc edge
  + creates additional downward ion wind for lift assist
  + demonstrated 18 N/m² in multi-stage configuration (MIT)
  + silent, no moving parts, complements piezo

Combined: piezo handles control and flow organization
          EHD handles raw lift supplement if needed

The MIT ionic wind plane flew with 3.3 N/m² over a 5-meter wingspan. A 30cm disc with optimized multi-stage EHD could contribute significant lift assist while the piezo skin handles all the fine control. The combination is still fully solid-state: no motors, no propellers, no moving parts in the conventional sense.

The disc drone has a vibrating boundary (skin) with a still center (electronics core) and the aperture is everywhere (the entire edge radiates). Both use phase and amplitude to create cymatic patterns in Φ. Both use resonant mode selection. Both convert electrical energy into organized field structure. The device converges energy inward through a point. The drone radiates organized energy outward through a surface. ⊛ and ✹, two sides of the same operation.
